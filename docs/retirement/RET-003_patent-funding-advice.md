## Analysis of the two funding paths

### Path 1 — Director's loan

**How it works:** Bret transfers personal funds (cash savings, ISA drawdown, or a personal overdraft) into Heirlooms Digital Ltd as a director's loan. The company records the liability on its balance sheet. Bret can repay himself later, interest-free or at HMRC's official rate (currently 2.25%), from company revenue or from a future capital event.

**The case for this path:**

1. **No dilution.** Bret currently owns 100 ordinary shares, representing 100% of Heirlooms Digital Ltd. An SEIS raise necessarily dilutes this. Even at the "non-dilutive in economic terms" framing Legal has used — which refers to the after-tax economics for the investor, not Bret's equity position — Bret's ownership percentage falls. That matters at exit.

2. **No governance obligations.** SEIS investors are shareholders. Even minority shareholders have rights: preemption rights on new share issues, information rights, and the ability to block certain decisions. At the seed stage, these are typically waived or minimised in well-drafted shareholder agreements — but they are not zero. Taking on an angel investor at this stage, before the product has revenue or even a filed patent, means Bret is negotiating from a weak position. The angel knows this.

3. **The company owes Bret the money back.** A director's loan is a recoverable asset for Bret. If the company succeeds, he repays himself from profit. If the company fails (bear scenario), the loan ranks as an unsecured creditor claim against the insolvent estate — Bret will likely recover nothing on failure. But it is not a personal liability: it doesn't hurt his credit rating, and it cannot be recovered from Bret personally in the way a personal guarantee on a bank loan could be.

4. **The amount is modest relative to the IP value upside.** The difference between having and not having the priority date is potentially hundreds of thousands of pounds in exit proceeds. Against a central-estimate uplift of £300,000–£900,000 to acquisition value, spending £10,000–£15,000 now is a strongly positive expected-value decision — provided the patent is eventually linked to a product with users and revenue. The risk is that the product never gets there, in which case the £10,000–£15,000 is a sunk cost.

**The case against this path:**

1. **Bret needs to have the personal funds.** The brief states he does not currently have funds "readily available." If Bret would need to draw down a stocks and shares ISA or pension to fund a director's loan, the tax and investment opportunity cost of that drawdown must be weighed. Selling equity ISA investments to fund a director's loan to a pre-revenue company is not, in general, good financial planning — it converts a tax-shielded investment into a unsecured loan to a speculative startup. However, if Bret has accessible cash savings (not ISA, not pension), this concern is much weaker.

2. **The loan creates a balance sheet liability for a very new company.** In year one of Heirlooms Digital Ltd's accounts, a £10,000–£15,000 director's loan liability alongside £0 revenue is the entire balance sheet. This is not a problem legally or commercially — it is entirely normal for an early-stage company — but it means the company cannot show healthy financials for at least 18–24 months.

3. **Repayment creates a future cash flow demand.** When the company does generate revenue, repaying the director's loan competes with other uses of that cash (further patent prosecution, legal costs, product development). This is manageable but should be planned.

**Bottom line on Path 1:** The director's loan is structurally the right choice. Its viability depends entirely on whether Bret can source the personal funds without damaging his own financial position. More on this in the questions section below.

### Path 2 — SEIS angel investment

**How it works:** Bret raises £20,000–£30,000 from one or two angels by issuing new shares in Heirlooms Digital Ltd. The investors receive SEIS relief: 50% income tax relief on the amount invested, plus CGT exemption on disposal. HMRC advance assurance takes 4–6 weeks. The angels take equity at a valuation to be negotiated.

**The case for this path:**

1. **No personal cash required from Bret.** If Bret genuinely cannot source £10,000–£15,000 personally without taking damaging personal financial steps, this is a material advantage. It removes personal financial risk from the immediate equation.

2. **Investor validation.** An angel who invests in a pre-revenue company is providing market validation. Legal is right that SEIS is economically generous to investors — the 50% income tax relief means a £15,000 investment costs a higher-rate taxpayer only £7,500 net. This makes angels more willing to take early risk. If the angels are well-chosen (people with relevant domain knowledge in care technology, legal tech, or cryptographic IP), they may provide more than money.

3. **Company could raise more than the minimum needed for the patent.** At £20,000–£30,000, the company has a buffer beyond the filing cost — funding could also cover initial PCT assessment, legal costs for IP assignments, and working capital for the first year of operations.

**The case against this path:**

1. **Dilution at the worst possible time.** The moment of maximum dilution pain is before a patent has been filed, before the product has users, and before revenue exists. An investor who puts in £25,000 at this stage will negotiate a lower valuation than they would if the patent were already filed, if there were 500 paying users, or if Care & Consent had an institutional client. Bret will give up more equity per pound raised right now than at any future point if the product continues to develop.

   To quantify: if Bret and an angel agree on a £125,000 post-money valuation (a very conservative seed valuation — a £25,000 investment at 20% equity), Bret's stake falls from 100% to 80%. In a base scenario exit at £1m–£7.2m (from RET-002), that 20% cost Bret £200,000–£1,440,000 in proceeds. Against a filing cost of £10,000–£15,000. That is between 13x and 96x the amount raised — a very expensive form of capital even after accounting for the tax relief the angel receives (which is the angel's benefit, not Bret's).

   Even at a higher pre-money valuation (say £200,000 pre-money, making a £25,000 investment approximately 11% equity), Bret is still giving up £110,000–£790,000 in potential base-scenario exit proceeds to fund a £10,000–£15,000 filing. The maths strongly disfavours SEIS unless the product is unlikely to reach meaningful exit proceeds regardless.

2. **4–6 weeks for HMRC advance assurance creates a disclosure window.** The UK patent priority date cannot wait for HMRC advance assurance to clear. If Bret needs to discuss the product — including the window capsule construction — with potential angels before the patent is filed, he is disclosing the construction without patent protection. Every angel meeting must be under NDA (Legal is correct on this), but NDAs are less robust than a filed patent. SEIS advance assurance and patent filing need to be sequenced carefully: file first, then approach angels. This means SEIS cannot fund the initial filing — it can only fund subsequent stages.

3. **Finding angels takes time.** Identifying angels who: (a) have the means to invest £10,000–£25,000, (b) qualify for SEIS relief (UK income taxpayer with no prior connection to the company), (c) understand the product and IP, (d) will accept the governance terms Bret sets, and (e) are available in the near term — is a non-trivial task. It is not weeks of work; it could be months. The patent filing window (mid-July 2026 target) is approximately 8 weeks from now. SEIS advance assurance alone takes 4–6 weeks. This timeline is extremely tight and carries meaningful execution risk.

4. **Governance drag.** Even a minority angel on a well-drafted SEIS shareholder agreement creates administrative overhead: shareholder resolutions, annual accounts, disclosure obligations. For a one-person operation already building a four-platform product in his own time, this overhead is a real cost in Bret's most constrained resource — time.

5. **JUXT employment consideration.** Bret is employed by JUXT. The LEG-005 outside interests declaration confirms JUXT's clause 17 requires written consent for outside business activities. Taking on external investors, even a small SEIS raise, elevates the profile and commercial ambition of Heirlooms Digital Ltd in ways that could attract more scrutiny from JUXT's People team than a purely personal project. This is not a showstopper, but it is a consideration — particularly given that the IP assignment deed (LEG-004) already creates some complexity around whether JUXT could claim any connection to work done during employment. The simpler the funding structure, the cleaner the JUXT declaration.

**Bottom line on Path 2:** SEIS is the wrong tool for funding the initial patent filing. It is expensive equity (in terms of dilution), it has a timeline incompatible with the patent urgency, it requires disclosure of the patent-sensitive construction to potential investors before the priority date is secured, and it imposes governance overhead on a solo founder. The only scenario in which SEIS makes sense for this purpose is if Bret genuinely cannot fund the UK filing through any other means — and even then, the sequencing problem (patent first, then investors) means SEIS cannot actually solve the immediate timing problem.

## Whether the patent IP is valuable enough to justify the spend at all

This is the right question to ask before either funding debate. My assessment:

**Yes — the UK filing cost is justified on expected-value grounds, with one important condition.**

The condition is that Bret believes the product has a realistic path to the base scenario from RET-002: 1,000–5,000 paying users over 3 years, leading to an exit in the £1m–£7.2m gross proceeds range. If that scenario is plausible — and I believe it is, based on the market analysis in RET-002 — then the patent adds £300,000–£900,000 to that exit value at the central estimate. Against a filing cost of £10,000–£15,000, the expected value of the patent exceeds its cost by a large multiple even at relatively low probabilities of reaching the base scenario.

**The bear scenario caveat:** If the product never reaches meaningful commercial scale, the patent's value collapses to its standalone asset value — perhaps £50,000–£200,000 if sold to a patent holding entity, net of any remaining prosecution costs. At that level, the £10,000–£15,000 filing cost is still recoverable, but only marginally. The patent is not a hedge against product failure; it is a multiplier on product success.

**The novelty window:** This is the most compelling argument for spending the money now rather than deferring. The Kavousi et al. paper (ASIACRYPT 2024) is already in the academic community. The drand/tlock ecosystem is active. As this space matures, the probability increases that another implementor reaches the same construction and files first. Once someone else establishes a priority date, Heirlooms' ability to file is permanently foreclosed. The cost of deferral is not merely delay — it is potential permanent loss of the IP position.

**PCT and national phase deferral is the correct cost management lever.** The immediate decision is whether to file in the UK to establish the priority date (£10,000–£15,000). The PCT (£5,500–£9,200) and national phase (£6,000–£16,000) decisions can wait 12 months and 30 months respectively. Framing the total 3–5 year cost of £30,000–£70,000 as the immediate decision is the wrong frame — only £10,000–£15,000 needs to be available right now.

## The deferral question: what is the strategic cost of waiting?

Legal has set a mid-July 2026 target. It is now 2026-05-17. That gives approximately 8–9 weeks.

**Can the filing be deferred — say, to Q1 2027 — to give Bret more time to accumulate the personal funds for a director's loan?**

I assess the deferral risk as real but not catastrophic in the short term, with one key caveat.

**The risk of deferring to Q1 2027 (approximately 7–8 months):**

- The novelty clock is not running on a fixed date — it runs from any public disclosure event. Provided the repository remains private, no external presentation is made, and no fundraising deck is shared, deferral to Q1 2027 does not inherently destroy the patentability.
- However, the risk of independent parallel development increases with time. The drand/tlock academic community is active. Someone else working in this space has a higher probability of reaching the same construction in 8 months than in 8 weeks.
- The priority date is the only thing Heirlooms gets from the filing on day one. Every month of delay is a month in which a competitor could establish a prior priority date.

**The risk that makes deferral genuinely dangerous:**

Any event that constitutes public disclosure — publishing a blog post, a podcast appearance where the construction is described, an investor pitch without NDA, or an accidental repo publication — permanently forecloses the UK patent position with no grace period. In the next 7–8 months, Bret may face pressure to talk about the product publicly (a beta launch, a press mention, a community post). The patent filing is the insurance against this risk. Every month without it is an uninsured month.

**My recommendation on deferral:** A short deferral of 2–4 weeks to allow Bret to confirm his personal funding position is acceptable and adds negligible risk. Deferral to Q1 2027 is a meaningful risk. If Bret cannot fund the UK filing through a director's loan within 4–8 weeks, the correct response is not to raise SEIS angels — it is to explore whether the patent attorney can stage their fees (some will defer payment to patent grant, or bill monthly), whether a smaller initial filing can establish the priority date at lower immediate cost, or whether a friend/family loan to the company (not Bret personally) can bridge the gap.

## Personal financial risk of a director's loan if the company fails

The director's loan creates a balance sheet liability for Heirlooms Digital Ltd, not a personal liability for Bret. Specifically:

- If Heirlooms Digital Ltd fails and is wound up or struck off, the director's loan is an unsecured creditor claim against the company. If there are no assets (likely in a bear scenario with no exit), Bret loses the loaned amount. This is a real cost — up to £15,000 — but it is a capital loss, not a personal debt.
- Bret's personal credit rating, his JUXT employment, and his personal assets (home, pension, ISA) are not at risk from the director's loan alone, provided he does not: (a) guarantee the company's debts personally, or (b) act as a director in a way that creates personal liability (wrongful trading etc — not a risk for a pre-revenue company winding down cleanly).
- HMRC requires that director's loans exceeding £10,000 that remain outstanding after 9 months from the company's year end are subject to an S455 tax charge of 33.75% on the outstanding loan amount (payable by the company). This charge is repayable to the company when the loan is repaid, but it creates a cash flow obligation on the company. For a company with zero revenue, this is manageable by keeping the loan repayment on a reasonable timeline — but Bret should be aware of this timing.

**Bottom line on personal financial risk:** A £10,000–£15,000 director's loan carries manageable personal financial risk. It is a loss of personal capital in the bear scenario. It is not a personal liability. This is very different from a personal loan (which creates personal debt regardless of company outcome) or from a personal guarantee on a company bank loan (which extends personal liability explicitly).

## Any other retirement-relevant considerations Legal may have missed

**1. The JUXT employment constraint creates a timing pressure Legal has not fully addressed.**

Bret is employed by JUXT under an agreement that requires written consent for outside business interests (clause 17, LEG-005). The JUXT outside interests declaration has been drafted and is pending CRN. Until JUXT's written consent is received, Heirlooms Digital Ltd is not fully regularised from an employment law perspective. This matters for the patent funding decision because:

- If JUXT were to dispute the declaration or withhold consent (unlikely, but possible), it would create uncertainty about IP ownership. LEG-004 (the IP assignment deed) warrants that the IP was created on Bret's own time, using his own resources, and was not created in the course of any employment. This is a factual question that depends on JUXT's interpretation of their employment agreement.
- The patent filing will name Heirlooms Digital Ltd as the assignee and Bret as the inventor. If JUXT subsequently claimed any of the IP, the patent could be challenged. This risk is low but not zero, and it should be resolved — ideally by having JUXT's written consent in hand — before the patent application is filed.

**Recommended sequencing:** CRN received → IP assignment deed signed → JUXT declaration sent → JUXT consent received → patent attorney engagement → patent filed. Doing the patent filing before JUXT consent creates a small but unnecessary gap.

**2. The timing of any SEIS raise has employment implications too.**

Taking on external investors changes the commercial character of Heirlooms Digital Ltd in a way that a purely personal director's loan does not. An SEIS raise could attract more scrutiny from JUXT than a bootstrapped operation. Legal should be aware of this dimension if SEIS is being considered.

**3. The opportunity cost of a personal loan should be compared against the opportunity cost of dilution, not presented as the only alternative.**

Legal's framing presents the choice as (a) director's loan requiring personal funds, or (b) SEIS angel. A third option — which Legal did not present — is a staged approach:

- File the UK application now using a director's loan at the smallest amount needed to establish the priority date and pay the attorney's initial retainer.
- Defer the bulk of attorney fees until the company has revenue or the PCT decision point arrives.
- Raise SEIS — if desired — at a later stage when the company has a filed patent, paying users, and a stronger negotiating position.

Some patent attorneys will agree to staged billing, particularly if they see a clear and well-documented IP case and an engaged client who understands the technology. The initial filing does not need to be a polished, prosecution-ready specification — it establishes priority. The heavy drafting work can follow. Bret should ask his patent attorney explicitly about payment staging.

**4. The pension angle: could this be a company contribution?**

This is a lateral thought, but relevant: once Heirlooms Digital Ltd has revenue, employer pension contributions are the most tax-efficient extraction route (no NI, deductible against corporation tax). The patent cost is a company expense, not a personal one — the correct structure is always for the company to fund it. The question is purely about where the company gets the initial capital from. Director's loan is the right answer to that question.

**5. Does the SEIS raise foreclose future fundraising at better terms?**

Yes, materially. SEIS investors who own equity (even 10–25%) complicate future fundraising because new investors will need to understand the existing cap table, the SEIS investors' rights, and whether their investment was structured to allow future dilution. A clean cap table (100% Bret) is the simplest and most attractive structure for any future strategic partner or acquirer. Every shareholder added before an exit is a name on the cap table who must agree to any deal structure.

## Recommended actions (immediate)

### 1. Establish Bret's personal funding position before any other decision

I need to know — and Legal should surface this question to Bret — whether he has accessible personal cash savings (not ISA, not pension) in the £10,000–£15,000 range. This single question determines whether the director's loan path is available without financial pain.

If Bret has the cash: director's loan. File the patent. End of discussion.

If Bret does not have the cash:
- Option A: Draw down a cash ISA (if held) — no tax impact, capital loss risk only.
- Option B: Draw down a stocks and shares ISA — triggers CGT if gains exceed the annual exempt amount; suboptimal but manageable.
- Option C: Ask the patent attorney about staged billing — initial retainer to establish priority date, balance on billing milestones.
- Option D: Very short-term overdraft or credit card advance to the company as director's loan — not ideal, but bridging £10,000–£15,000 for 3–6 months while the company generates first revenue is a known approach for sole-director startups.
- Option E: Family or friend loan into the company (not to Bret personally) — structurally similar to SEIS without the equity dilution. If a trusted individual can lend £10,000–£15,000 to the company, with a formal loan agreement for repayment (at HMRC official rate or a commercially agreed rate), this provides funding without equity dilution and without HMRC advance assurance delays.

What Bret should not do: raise SEIS to fund the UK filing.

### 2. File the UK patent before approaching any investor

Regardless of funding path chosen: the patent must be filed before Bret discusses the window capsule construction with any angel investor. Every investor meeting before the priority date is a disclosure risk. This sequencing is non-negotiable.

### 3. Defer PCT to the 12-month mark; defer national phase to the 30-month mark

The total £30,000–£70,000 cost is a 3–5 year spend, not an immediate one. Framing it as an immediate funding need creates false urgency and could push Bret into a premature SEIS raise. The immediate need is £10,000–£15,000 for the UK filing. Budget accordingly.

### 4. Revisit SEIS at the 12-month mark with a much stronger hand

SEIS is not necessarily always the wrong answer — it is wrong right now, for this purpose, at this stage. If, in 12 months' time, the product has:
- A filed UK patent (priority date secured)
- 500+ paying users
- A Care & Consent MVP in private beta
- Demonstrable revenue trajectory

...then raising £50,000–£100,000 at a pre-money valuation of £500,000–£1,000,000 on SEIS terms would represent capital at a materially lower dilution cost. Bret would give up 5–17% rather than 11–25%, and the SEIS investors would be buying into a business with demonstrable traction. That is a healthy fundraising conversation. This conversation in May 2026 is not.
