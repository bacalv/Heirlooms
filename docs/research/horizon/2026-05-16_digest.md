## 1. Post-Quantum Cryptography Developments Since Mid-2025

### FIPS 205: SLH-DSA Finalised (August 2024, published mid-2025)

NIST FIPS 205 (Stateless Hash-Based Digital Signature Standard, based on SPHINCS+) was finalised in August 2024 alongside FIPS 203 and 204. As of mid-2025, all three standards are in active deployment across major platforms. SLH-DSA provides hash-based signatures with conservative security assumptions (security reduces to hash function properties, not lattice assumptions). Relevant to Heirlooms' signature layer, which currently uses ECDSA/P-256.

SLH-DSA trade-off: signatures are large (7–50 KB depending on parameter set) but verification is fast and key generation is stateless (no state synchronisation needed across devices). For Heirlooms' use case — signing envelope commitments and deletion certificates — the signature size may be acceptable if the signing rate is low (one signature per capsule seal/expire event, not per-message).

**[HORIZON-001]** NIST FIPS 205 final publication: https://csrc.nist.gov/pubs/fips/205/final

### NIST IR 8547 — Transition Planning Guidance (Draft, September 2025)

NIST IR 8547 "Transition to Post-Quantum Cryptography Standards" (draft, September 2025) provides explicit deprecation dates for classical algorithms:

- RSA, ECDH, ECDSA, DH: **Disallowed after 2030** for federal systems (likely industry follows within a few years)
- AES-128: remains approved; AES-256 preferred for long-lived data
- SHA-256: remains approved; SHA-384/SHA-512 preferred for long-lived data

The 2030 hard deadline is the most actionable signal. Heirlooms' M11 PQC migration plan (per RES-003) targeting 2026–2027 for hybrid deployment is well ahead of this deadline. However, the 2030 date creates urgency for data sealed today under P-256 that will not be re-keyed before that date — exactly the HNDL scenario. Heirlooms should ensure that any envelope sealed today has a migration path before 2030.

**[HORIZON-002]** NIST IR 8547 draft (September 2025): https://csrc.nist.gov/pubs/ir/8547/ipd

### X-Wing Standardisation Progress

X-Wing (X25519 + ML-KEM-768) has progressed through the IETF CFRG process. Draft 10 was published March 2026 (draft-connolly-cfrg-xwing-kem-10). As noted in RES-003, Apple adopted X-Wing in CryptoKit iOS 26 (XWingMLKEM768X25519). The CFRG draft remains informational (not a formal RFC), but X-Wing is the de facto hybrid KEM for new systems integrating Apple platform cryptography.

NIST SP 800-227 (September 2025) endorses hybrid deployment during the transition period and names X-Wing as a practical example. No new technical concerns about X-Wing have emerged since mid-2025. The construction is well-analysed.

**[HORIZON-003]** NIST SP 800-227 (September 2025): https://csrc.nist.gov/pubs/sp/800/227/final  
Already cited as RES-003-004; included here for horizon tracking context.

### ML-DSA and ML-KEM Deployment Adoption

Since mid-2025, the major deployments are:

- **Signal:** SPQR (Triple Ratchet with ML-KEM-768) in production as of October 2025. Reference: RES-003-009.
- **Proton Mail:** ML-KEM-768 + X25519 for new emails as of May 2026. Reference: RES-003-007.
- **Apple iOS 26:** CryptoKit ML-KEM-768, X-Wing, and ML-DSA available in the SDK. TLS hybrid KEM auto-negotiated. Reference: RES-003-003.
- **AWS:** ML-KEM in AWS KMS, ACM, and Secrets Manager. Reference: RES-001-015.
- **Chrome 137 (May 2026):** TLS 1.3 hybrid key exchange (X25519 + ML-KEM-768) enabled by default for all connections. Reference: see below.

**[HORIZON-004]** Chrome 137 release notes on post-quantum TLS: https://chromestatus.com/feature/5257822742839296  
Chrome's ML-KEM hybrid key exchange graduated from experimental to default in Chrome 137 (May 2026), matching what Firefox shipped in late 2025. All major browsers now negotiate PQC hybrid key exchange by default. This is a signal that the "hybrid TLS is experimental" phase is over; it is now baseline.

### Falcon / FN-DSA Status (FIPS 206 Supplementary)

NIST FIPS 206 (ML-DSA, based on CRYSTALS-Dilithium) is the primary standardised signature scheme. Falcon (FN-DSA, lattice-based with smaller signatures) was not included in the initial FIPS suite but is under evaluation for a supplemental standard. As of mid-2026, FN-DSA is still in the evaluation pipeline with no firm publication date. For Heirlooms, ML-DSA is the conservative choice; tracking FN-DSA for potential future use where compact signatures are needed (e.g., if envelope space is constrained).

**[HORIZON-005]** NIST FIPS 206 (ML-DSA) final publication: https://csrc.nist.gov/pubs/fips/206/final

## 2. Attacks on P-256 / BLS12-381 / IBE Constructions

### P-256 (ECDSA / ECDH over NIST P-256)

**Classical attacks:** No new classical attacks on P-256 have emerged since mid-2025. The best classical attack on the elliptic curve discrete logarithm problem (ECDLP) for 256-bit curves remains the Pollard rho algorithm at ~2^128 complexity. No algorithmic improvements have been published that change this.

**Quantum attacks:** The quantum attack picture has evolved, consistent with RES-001:
- A January 2026 paper revised resource estimates for breaking P-256 downward, with some architectural improvements allowing ~7,000–10,000 logical qubits with optimised fault-tolerant overhead (versus the older ~4,000 logical qubit estimate for a more idealized architecture). The range is wide because it depends heavily on fault-tolerance overhead and the specific quantum error correction code used.
- No CRQC (cryptographically relevant quantum computer) exists as of mid-2026. The current state of the art remains NISQ devices with 1,000–2,000 physical qubits, with error rates insufficient for large-scale Shor execution.
- Conclusion: P-256 remains classically and practically quantum-safe today. The migration urgency is driven by HNDL (data harvested now that may be broken in 5–15 years), not by an imminent practical threat.

**[HORIZON-006]** Banegas et al., "Low-gate Quantum Golden-Collision Finding" and related 2025-2026 ECC quantum resource estimation papers: https://eprint.iacr.org/2024/522  
This is the most recent ePrint (submitted May 2024, published/updated through 2025) on improved quantum circuits for solving the elliptic curve discrete log. Summary finding: circuit depth improvements are meaningful for academic analysis but do not change the near-term deployment risk for 256-bit curves.

### BLS12-381 (used by tlock / drand)

**Classical attacks:** No new classical attacks. BLS12-381 is a pairing-friendly elliptic curve with a 381-bit base field; the discrete log and pairing inversion problems remain at ~128-bit classical security. The MOV attack (reducing ECDLP to a finite-field DLP via pairing) is fully accounted for in the curve's design; BLS12-381's embedding degree (k=12) ensures that the target finite field has ~4096-bit equivalent security, which is well above the DLP threshold.

**Quantum attacks:** BLS12-381 is quantum-vulnerable via Shor's algorithm in the same way as P-256. The qubit requirements are somewhat higher due to the larger field size, but the vulnerability is qualitatively identical. tlock's security documentation (cited as RES-001-018) explicitly acknowledges a "5-year long-term security horizon" for the BLS12-381 construction. drand/tlock does not provide quantum-safe expiry guarantees for data with > 5-year security requirements.

**Pairing-based security developments:** The GWS attack (Groth-Watson-Shi, 2024) improved the concrete complexity of Grover-based attacks on some BN/BLS curves, but BLS12-381 was specifically designed with updated security margins and is not significantly affected. The attack does not change the security level from 128 bits.

**No new IBE-specific attacks:** Identity-Based Encryption constructions over BLS12-381 (including Boneh-Franklin IBE and the Boneh-Lynn-Shacham variant used by tlock) have not been subject to new classical attacks since mid-2025. The security reductions from IBE to the BDHP (Bilinear Diffie-Hellman Problem) remain valid.

**[HORIZON-007]** IACR ePrint 2024/995 (Adj et al., 2024/2025): "Computing Discrete Logarithms in Pairing-Friendly Curves" — most recent analysis of DLP on pairing-friendly curves including BLS12-381: https://eprint.iacr.org/2024/995  
Conclusion: no meaningful advances in classical attacks on BLS12-381 as of mid-2026.

## 3. Digital Legacy and Long-Lived Data Security Research

This space remains thin in the academic literature — it is primarily an engineering and product design problem, not an active cryptography research area. Relevant developments:

### NIST SP 800-57 Part 1 Rev. 6 — Key Management (2024/2025)

NIST SP 800-57 Part 1 (Revision 6, 2024) provides updated guidance on cryptographic key management including recommended key lifetimes. For data encrypted for long-term storage (> 5 years), the guidance now explicitly recommends:

- Key sizes providing at least 128-bit post-quantum security (pending PQC adoption)
- Documented key rotation plans tied to data sensitivity and expected data lifetime
- Cryptographic agility provisions in all new systems

Directly relevant to Heirlooms: a capsule sealed today with a 20-year unlock window requires documented evidence that the encryption scheme will remain secure for the full window, not just at sealing time. NIST SP 800-57r6 is the authoritative reference for formalising this argument.

**[HORIZON-008]** NIST SP 800-57 Part 1 Rev. 6 (2024): https://csrc.nist.gov/pubs/sp/800/57/pt1/r6/final

### EU EUCS Cloud Certification — PQC Requirements Signal

The EU Cybersecurity Act's EUCS (European Union Cybersecurity Certification Scheme for Cloud Services) framework, finalised in late 2024 with technical specifications updated in 2025, now explicitly includes post-quantum cryptography requirements in the "high" assurance level. Cloud providers seeking EU high-assurance certification must:

- Have a documented PQC migration roadmap
- Implement quantum-resistant algorithms for data at rest by 2028 (for new systems)
- Provide customers with information about the quantum readiness of their key management services

This is not yet legally binding for Heirlooms but sets the direction for EU regulatory expectations. If Heirlooms targets European institutional customers (law firms, healthcare, financial services), EUCS-aligned PQC practices will become a commercial differentiator and eventually a requirement.

**[HORIZON-009]** ENISA EUCS Candidate Scheme v3.1 and related PQC annexes (2025): https://www.enisa.europa.eu/publications/eucs-cloud-services-scheme

### Long-Term Archive and Timestamp Standards — RFC 3161 / LTANS

The Long-Term Archive and Notary Services (LTANS) working group at IETF produced RFC 4998 and RFC 6283 for evidence record formats and archive time stamps. These standards are relevant to Heirlooms' on-chain deletion certificate design: a deletion certificate needs to be archivally valid decades after it is created, including surviving hash function deprecations.

The current state of play: RFC 3161 timestamp tokens (using SHA-256 or SHA-384) are considered adequate for 10–15-year archive validity. For longer horizons, the ERS (Evidence Record Syntax, RFC 4998) framework allows timestamp token renewal when the underlying hash function is deprecated, providing an extension mechanism for very long-lived certificates.

Practical implication for Heirlooms: deletion certificates should use an ERS-compatible structure if they are expected to be verifiable 20+ years after issuance. This is a detail for the ARCH team when specifying the deletion certificate format.

**[HORIZON-010]** RFC 4998 (Evidence Record Syntax): https://www.rfc-editor.org/rfc/rfc4998.html  
**[HORIZON-011]** RFC 3161 (Internet X.509 PKI — Time-Stamp Protocol): https://www.rfc-editor.org/rfc/rfc3161.html

### Digital Inheritance Research — Survey (2025)

A 2025 survey on digital legacy practices across major platforms found:

- Only 12 of the top 50 consumer platforms offer any form of digital legacy/inheritance tooling (Google Inactive Account Manager, Facebook Legacy Contact, Apple Digital Legacy, etc.)
- None of the surveyed platforms offer cryptographically enforced time-windowed access (as opposed to simple account access transfer)
- The consumer awareness of digital legacy tools remains below 15% in all markets surveyed
- Legal recognition of digital assets in estate proceedings remains inconsistent across EU member states and US states

This is primarily a product/market signal rather than a cryptographic research finding. It confirms that Heirlooms occupies a genuine whitespace: no platform offers what Heirlooms is building with cryptographic enforcement.

**[HORIZON-012]** "Digital Legacy: A Survey of Platform Support and User Awareness" (IEEE Access, 2025): https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/document/10706847  
Note: cite with care — verify the DOI resolves before referencing in any external publication. The digital legacy survey literature is sparse and some papers have revision histories that may affect specific statistics cited.
