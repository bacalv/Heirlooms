## Brand reframe recommendations

### The reframe in plain terms

The founding narrative positioned Heirlooms around death, inheritance, and posthumous delivery. That framing is emotionally true for a subset of use cases — the deepest, most powerful ones — but it is not the whole product. The reframe that emerged in the 2026-05-16 session is the right one: Heirlooms is a **time-based digital archive**. The gap between now and a future moment of meaning is the product's territory. Death is one shape that gap takes. A message sealed for a daughter's 18th birthday is another. A brand campaign that unlocks over three days is another.

This reframe does not diminish the grief-aware, posthumous use case. It situates it as the deepest expression of something the product does across every segment. The brand voice — solemn, dignified, warm — survives the reframe intact. What changes is the pitch to the outside world.

### Vocabulary to retire

| Word / phrase | Why | Replacement |
|---|---|---|
| "loss" | Positions Heirlooms as a grief product; limits addressable market | Remove. Context will carry grief-awareness where needed. |
| "cloud storage" | Positions as infrastructure; kills differentiation | Never use. Heirlooms is not infrastructure. |
| "backup" | Same as above — utility framing | Never use. |
| "syncing" | Same category as backup | Never use. |
| "share your photos" | Social media framing; cheapens the product | Never use without a qualifying intention phrase. |
| "dead man's switch" | Technical jargon with a morbid flavour | "Inactivity-triggered unlock" or simply "if you're no longer here" |

### Vocabulary to add or formalise

| Word / phrase | Use | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| "time-based digital archive" | Category name for investor / press context | Not necessarily for consumer copy — too abstract. |
| "window" | The bounded time period between unlock and expiry | A capsule "has a window." This is precise and distinctive. |
| "cryptographically enforced" | Consumer and legal copy | Distinguish from "we promise." |
| "bloomed" | Already in BRAND.md — promote to marketing copy | The moment a capsule delivers is a bloom, not just a notification. |
| "sealed" | Already in BRAND.md — promote to marketing copy | The moment of cryptographic commitment. |
| "executor" | Legal and Care Mode contexts | Precise; avoid softening to "trusted contact" in legal segments. |
| "garden" | Consumer voice for the vault — already in BRAND.md | Maintain discipline: do not let other segments dilute this term. |
| "chained capsule" | Technical notation for Experience segment | Keep as internal terminology; translate for each audience. |

### BRAND.md changes recommended

Three additions are needed in BRAND.md to reflect this session's thinking. The Marketing Director recommends raising these with the CTO before writing them into the document:

1. **Add a "Platform positioning" section** that codifies "time-based digital archive" as the category claim and specifies which contexts it is appropriate (investor, press, partner pitches) versus where consumer-friendly alternatives should be used ("the only vault that keeps a promise until the moment it is meant to be opened").

2. **Extend the banned vocabulary list** to include "loss," "grief platform," and "digital legacy tool" — these are category-limiting and invite comparison with bereavement services rather than secure time-based products.

3. **Add vocabulary for the Experience segment** without allowing it to contaminate the Memory Archive voice. The Experience segment needs its own tone register — more playful, competitive, campaign-oriented — that lives clearly under the Heirlooms brand umbrella without crossing into the garden metaphor. Suggested approach: Experience segment copy uses the sealed/bloomed vocabulary but replaces "garden" with "vault" (neutral, technical) when addressing campaign designers and agencies.

## Three-segment model

### Segment names

The working names from the session — Memory Archive, Care & Consent, Experience — are directionally right. I am refining them slightly for commercial clarity:

| Working name | Recommended name | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Memory Archive | **Memory Archive** | Strong. Accurate. Emotionally resonant without being morbid. Keep. |
| Care & Consent | **Care & Consent** | Precise and legally credible. Keep. The ampersand signals two-party involvement — consent is not just assumed. |
| Experience | **Experience** | Holds the space between ARG, brand campaign, and serialised storytelling. Keep — but note that "Experience" risks conflating with "customer experience" in enterprise contexts; may need qualification as "Experience Platform" in B2B pitches. |

### Who is the customer and what does the purchase decision look like?

**Memory Archive**

- Customer: individual consumer, 35–65, typically a parent, grandparent, or person facing a significant life event (illness, retirement, milestone birthday).
- Purchase trigger: emotional — "I want to ensure something survives me, or reaches the people I love at the right moment."
- Decision speed: slow. This is not an impulse purchase. Users often return weeks after discovery. The PLG / friend-tester model is important here — the product must be experienced before it is purchased.
- Payment model: annual subscription, likely £5–12/month equivalent. Annual billing preferred (cash flow, churn reduction). Could tier on storage volume, but the more compelling tier axis is cryptographic guarantees — basic vs. full E2EE, or inclusion of executor/social recovery configuration.
- Key objection to address: "What happens to my data if Heirlooms closes?" — needs a prominent, credible answer at point of purchase, not buried in terms.

**Care & Consent**

- Customer: two parties. The Power of Attorney holder (the buyer; makes the purchase decision, often a middle-aged adult child or professional solicitor) and the person being cared for (the subject; must consent while they retain capacity).
- Purchase trigger: pragmatic and urgent. A family member has received a diagnosis (Alzheimer's, other degenerative conditions) and the family is trying to establish arrangements while capacity exists. The emotional component is high but the framing is practical: "we need to do this now."
- Decision speed: moderate. Often paired with other care planning activity (updating will, financial LPA). Partnership with solicitors and estate planning advisers is the primary acquisition channel — this segment does not arrive via app store browsing.
- Payment model: family subscription, with a professional tier for solicitors or care homes managing multiple clients. Professional tier is a B2B sale — annual contract, invoiced. Pricing should reflect the legal weight of what is being set up, not compete with consumer SaaS on price.
- Key objection to address: "Is this legally recognised?" — Marketing cannot claim legal validity; the Legal persona must produce copy-safe language. The claim Heirlooms can make is that consent is cryptographically recorded at the point it is given, with a timestamp and a cryptographic commitment, which is a stronger evidentiary trail than a paper form.

**Experience**

- Customer: brand campaign agencies, ARG designers, publishers (serialised fiction), event organisers, and — later — white-label enterprise clients. The buyer is a creative director, brand strategist, or product manager, not an individual consumer.
- Purchase trigger: creative/commercial. "We want to do something no one has done before with timed revelation and cryptographic authenticity."
- Decision speed: depends on campaign timeline. Branded campaigns can move in weeks if there is budget authority; enterprise white-label is a 3–6 month sales cycle.
- Payment model: campaign fee (one-time, per campaign), or platform licensing (annual, for agencies that run recurring campaigns). A self-serve lower tier (small ARG designers, indie publishers) is valuable for PLG and case-study generation. White-label/org-flavoured app is a separate premium offering — requires bespoke development and support.
- Key objection to address: "How does this fit the Heirlooms brand?" — agencies will want to understand whether their campaign content has to live inside the Heirlooms consumer experience. The answer should be no: white-label and org-flavoured configurations create a distinct branded surface. The cryptographic infrastructure is Heirlooms; the consumer face can be the client's brand.

## USP mapping by segment

### The five core USPs

From the 2026-05-16 session, five USPs were identified:

1. Cryptographic enforcement — the promise is mathematical, not operational
2. Time-windowed delivery — both a lower bound (not before) and an upper bound (not after)
3. Post-quantum architecture — built for a 40-year horizon, not next year
4. Chained capsule DAG — conditional, sequential, competitive delivery
5. E2EE with zero-knowledge server — Heirlooms cannot read your content, ever

### Mapping by segment

| USP | Memory Archive | Care & Consent | Experience |
|---|---|---|---|
| Cryptographic enforcement | Primary — "We cannot read your capsule. The date is the gate, not our word." | Primary — "Consent is recorded cryptographically. The timestamp is immutable." | Supporting — "The reveal is real. No one, including us, can open it early." |
| Time-windowed delivery | Primary — milestone delivery is the product's deepest promise | Secondary — window is a care monitoring feature (check-in windows, not milestone delivery) | Primary — the window IS the mechanic. Unlock windows, competitive delivery, expiry-as-death. |
| Post-quantum architecture | Supporting — "Your capsule sealed for 2039 is protected today against threats that don't exist yet." | Supporting — same as Memory Archive | Not primary in pitch — agencies don't care about crypto primitives |
| Chained capsule DAG | Not relevant to most consumers (deferred to advanced tier, if at all) | Not relevant | Primary — this IS what makes Experience unique |
| E2EE zero-knowledge server | Primary — "Heirlooms staff cannot read your content. Ever." | Primary — E2EE is a legal and ethical prerequisite for care monitoring | Supporting — agencies care, but it's a trust signal, not a headline |

### USPs that emerge only from segment combination

One cross-segment USP emerges that is not present in any single segment: **the platform as a trustworthy host for things that must not be seen too early and must not survive too long.** A brand that uses the Experience platform for a campaign could also discover — or be shown — that the same infrastructure that ran their ARG can also protect their executive communications, document escrow, or legacy planning. The Experience segment is a low-friction entry into Heirlooms for enterprise clients who would never have considered a "digital legacy" product.

A second cross-segment USP: **consent established while capacity exists, used in a care context, is a fundamentally different legal instrument than consent inferred from a form**. The Marketing Director is not positioned to make this legal claim without Legal persona review, but if it is validated, it is a premium differentiator for the Care & Consent segment that has no equivalent in any competitor product visible in the market today.

## Care Mode positioning

### The core tension

Care Mode monitors someone. Even when consent is cryptographic, explicit, and revocable, the word "monitoring" triggers surveillance associations. The product must be positioned around the thing that makes it not surveillance: consent that precedes the loss of capacity, cryptographically committed to and revocable while capacity remains. This is closer to an advance directive than a tracking tool.

### Recommended positioning frame

Do not use the word "monitoring." Use "care access" or "care window." The distinction: monitoring implies ongoing observation; a care window implies a pre-agreed gate that opens only under defined conditions.

Candidate positioning: **"Heirlooms Care Mode: the access your family agrees to together, before they need it."** This frames the product around the agreement, not the surveillance. It is forward-looking (before capacity is lost) rather than reactive (after something goes wrong).

Secondary positioning for the legal/professional audience: **"Cryptographically recorded consent. Revocable while capacity exists. Timestamped and tamper-evident."** This speaks to solicitors, care home managers, and LPA professionals in language they understand.

### Brand risk of surveillance association

The risk is real but manageable if the product is positioned correctly from the outset. The positioning must do three things:

1. Lead with consent, not monitoring. Every word about Care Mode should start with the person being cared for, not the Power of Attorney holder. The subject's agency is the story.
2. Emphasise revocability. "You can cancel this at any time while you have capacity" must be visible at the moment of setup, not buried in settings. This is a trust signal, not a caveat.
3. Never describe the product as a "tracker" or use location, activity, or health monitoring language, even if such features are added later. The vocabulary of surveillance — even benign surveillance — will contaminate the brand in the Memory Archive and Experience segments.

### Handoff required

The Psychologist persona should review the Care Mode UX design before any public positioning is finalised. The grief-aware UX work that applies to posthumous delivery also applies here — the product is entering a person's most vulnerable period, and every word and interaction pattern matters. The Marketing Director can set the commercial frame; the Psychologist should validate that the emotional experience of setting up Care Mode does not cause harm.

## Experience segment commercial model

### Revenue model options

Three models are viable, not mutually exclusive:

**Option A — Campaign fee model**
A one-time fee per campaign, priced by scale (number of recipients, number of capsules, campaign duration). Comparable to event software or experiential agency tooling. Suitable for brand campaigns and one-off ARG productions. Predictable for the buyer; unpredictable for Heirlooms (revenue is lumpy and depends on deal flow).

**Option B — Platform licensing**
Annual license fee for agencies that run recurring campaigns. Access to the chained capsule builder, a campaign dashboard, and a self-serve recipient management layer. Recurring revenue; better for Heirlooms' financials. Requires enough agency adoption to justify the investment in a self-serve B2B layer.

**Option C — White-label / API tier**
Bespoke contract for organisations that want Heirlooms infrastructure under their own brand. Publishers running serialised content drops, games companies running ARGs, brands running loyalty programs. Highest revenue per customer; longest sales cycle; most engineering support required. Should be sequenced after Option A or B is generating inbound demand.

**Recommended sequencing:**
Start with Option A to generate case studies and prove the model. Move to Option B as recurring agency relationships develop. Open Option C to inbound requests only — do not sell it proactively until the engineering support burden is understood.

### Competitive landscape

The Experience segment sits at the intersection of several existing categories, none of which has solved the cryptographic enforcement problem:

- **ARG toolkits** (RSVP.ly, Conductor, bespoke agency builds): experience the "puzzle hunt" side but have no cryptographic time-gate; reveal timing is operationally managed, not mathematically enforced.
- **Email scheduling tools** (Mailchimp, Boomerang): deliver on a date but are trivially bypassable and have no chaining or conditional delivery.
- **Event/experience platforms** (Eventbrite, Splash): manage attendees but have no cryptographic content delivery layer.
- **NFT drops / Web3 tooling**: attempted cryptographic scarcity but with enormous friction (wallets, gas fees) and no mainstream adoption outside crypto-native audiences.

Heirlooms' differentiation in this space: cryptographic enforcement of the window (not just scheduling), true expiry (content that no one can recover after the window closes), and chaining (one capsule's completion unlocks another). No competitor combines all three.

### Who are the buyers?

- **Brand campaign agencies**: creative directors seeking a "first" for a campaign. These are relatively accessible via cold outreach and award entries. A successful case study in this segment is worth significant earned media.
- **ARG designers**: a small community but influential and technically sophisticated. They will understand the cryptographic claims and will probe them. If the product withstands that scrutiny, they will become advocates. Target via niche channels (ARG Net, puzzle hunt communities, Kickstarter campaign creators).
- **Publishers**: serialised fiction with timed chapter reveals, interactive mystery experiences. Publishers with established readerships are a medium-term opportunity; independent authors on Substack or Kickstarter are an earlier, higher-velocity opportunity.
- **Games companies**: particularly indie studios running marketing campaigns or in-game ARG tie-ins. Enterprise games companies are a longer-term, higher-value opportunity.

## Chained capsules — top commercial applications

The chained capsule DAG mechanic (notation: `C₁({A,B}, [T₀+2d→T₀+3d], {puzzle, ref→C₂}) → C₂({winner}, [T₀+4d→T₀+5d], {prize})`) introduces four capabilities that have no equivalent in any product the Marketing Director is aware of:

1. **Time-windowed delivery** — a capsule is only deliverable between T₀+n and T₀+m; after T₀+m it expires cryptographically.
2. **Competitive delivery** — the first solver of a puzzle capsule receives the next capsule; all others receive nothing (or a participation consolation, if designed that way).
3. **Expiry-as-death** — a second capsule in a chain that receives no completions from the first capsule is never delivered; it dies with the unsolved puzzle. This mechanic alone is commercially unique.
4. **QR codes as capsule references** — physical artifacts (magazine inserts, product packaging, event programmes) can carry a QR code that resolves to a Heirlooms capsule, bridging physical and digital campaign delivery.

### Top five commercial applications (ranked by near-term addressability)

**1. ARG / puzzle hunt campaigns**
Immediately addressable. The community exists, is active, and will understand the value proposition without extensive education. A single well-executed ARG built on Heirlooms chained capsules — with the expiry mechanic genuinely operative — is a proof-of-concept that sells the next campaign. Target: indie ARG designers and Kickstarter-funded puzzle games.

**2. Brand reveal campaigns**
A product launch where the reveal is gated behind a time window and competitive engagement. The brand cannot "accidentally" reveal early; the cryptographic gate is a genuine story. Magazine / packaging QR codes with limited "first 300 unlock codes" are a near-term execution. Target: brand campaign agencies with experiential marketing budgets.

**3. Serialised fiction with timed chapter unlocks**
An author releases a novel in chapters, each chapter unlocking on a specific date, with early completion of an embedded puzzle unlocking the next chapter faster. Expiry-as-death means unsolved chapters are genuinely gone. Target: Substack authors, Kickstarter fiction campaigns, interactive fiction publishers.

**4. Loyalty / membership programs with cryptographic scarcity**
A brand offers a series of exclusive drops accessible only during a window; completion of one drop's engagement unlocks access to the next drop. The scarcity is real, not simulated. Target: brands with existing loyalty programs seeking a credible "digital collectible" mechanic without the Web3 baggage.

**5. Corporate comms / document escrow with timed release**
Board documents, investor communications, and M&A materials that must be released at a specific moment and must provably not have been accessible before that moment. The audience is legal and compliance teams, not consumers. Target: enterprise, via legal partnerships (the Legal persona should flag this as a long-term opportunity with high per-deal value).

### Expiry-as-death as a headline feature

Of all the chained capsule mechanics, expiry-as-death is the most commercially unique and the hardest to replicate. "If no one solves it in time, it is gone forever. Even we cannot retrieve it." This claim — if the cryptographic architecture supports it — is a genuine first in commercial digital experience design. It should be the centrepiece of the Experience segment pitch.

## Friend-tester / PLG strategy

### Current state

The friend-tester model is currently organic: Bret's personal contacts are testing the product in exchange for free storage. This is working but is not a strategy — it is a favour economy. Formalising it into a PLG (product-led growth) motion requires answering three questions: what is the free tier offer, what is the conversion trigger, and what mechanism drives referral?

### Recommended free tier design

**Offer:** Free storage for verified testers, in exchange for structured feedback (a brief session-end survey, or access to crash reports and analytics with explicit consent). Frame this as "founding member" access, not "beta testing" — the vocabulary matters. Testers who feel they are founding members have skin in the game; testers who feel they are QA labour do not.

**Storage limit:** Generous enough to experience the product's core value (uploading a meaningful collection, creating and sealing a capsule). Suggested: 5GB or 50 photos/videos, whichever comes first. This is enough to feel the product, not enough to live in it indefinitely.

**Conversion trigger:** The natural conversion moment is the first time a user wants to seal a capsule for a real recipient — someone who is not them. At that moment, the stakes become real: "This person I love will receive this. I want it protected properly." The paid tier should be positioned at that moment: "To make this cryptographically permanent for [name], upgrade to Heirlooms." The word "cryptographically" is not jargon here — it is the emotional payoff of the product.

**Referral mechanic:** Free-tier users who invite a friend who creates an account and uploads at least one photo extend their free tier by three months. This is a referral reward, not a pyramid scheme — it rewards genuine product sharing, not email blasting. The "one photo" threshold ensures the referred user has experienced the product.

### What formalising this looks like

1. A documented "Founding Member" program with explicit terms (what free tier includes, what is expected in return, when the free period ends).
2. An in-product prompt at the conversion trigger moment.
3. A structured feedback mechanism (even if it is just a Typeform for now) so the data from testers is actually captured and used.
4. A small CRM layer — even a spreadsheet — tracking who each tester is, when they joined, and whether they converted. The PA should own this until there is enough volume to justify tooling.

The friend-tester program is Heirlooms' lowest-cost acquisition channel and its most credible word-of-mouth source. It should be formalised before any paid marketing is considered.

## Brand risk assessment

### The core tension: solemn/dignified voice meets gamification and competition

The Experience segment introduces mechanics — puzzles, competitive delivery, expiry-as-death — that are fundamentally playful and competitive. These sit in direct tension with the brand voice established in BRAND.md: solemn, dignified, grief-aware. The Marketing Director's assessment is that this tension is real but not irresolvable, provided two conditions are met.

**Condition 1: Segment audiences must not encounter each other by accident.**
A Memory Archive user who discovers that their "digital legacy vault" is also running brand campaigns for energy drinks will experience cognitive dissonance. This is not a hypothetical — it is a specific UX and brand design problem. The Experience segment must operate in a clearly distinct product surface (org-flavoured app, white-label configuration, or at minimum a clearly labelled "for campaigns" section of the product) that a Memory Archive user never sees unless they are looking for it.

**Condition 2: The brand vocabulary must hold across both segments.**
The words "sealed," "bloomed," "window," and "cryptographic" must mean the same thing whether used in a memory capsule for a grieving parent or a brand campaign for a product launch. The brand is the cryptographic promise; the tone is what varies by segment. This is achievable but requires discipline — the Experience segment must not introduce game vocabulary ("win," "lose," "prize") into the shared brand layer. These words live in the Experience segment's own tone register, which is a sub-register of Heirlooms, not a replacement for it.

### Are the audiences separable?

Yes, structurally — but only with deliberate product architecture decisions. The recommendation is:

- **Consumer Memory Archive and Care & Consent share a product surface.** Their voice registers are close enough (solemn, dignified, trust-focused) that they can coexist in a single product without brand dissonance. Care Mode users are a subset of Memory Archive users in many cases.

- **Experience is a distinct surface.** Whether that is a white-label app, a separate subdomain (`experience.heirlooms.digital`), or a clearly segmented "for organisations" product tier, it must not be the default surface for a consumer who finds Heirlooms via word-of-mouth about digital legacy.

### Does serving Experience dilute the core brand?

The risk is not inherent in the product mechanic — chained capsules and time-windows are extensions of the same core cryptographic promise. The risk is in the marketing and communications layer: if Heirlooms is described publicly as "a platform for ARGs and brand campaigns," it will struggle to acquire Memory Archive consumers who need to trust the product with their deepest intentions.

The recommended public narrative: **Heirlooms is a time-based digital archive that makes promises permanent.** Memory Archive, Care & Consent, and Experience are three expressions of the same underlying capability. This framing keeps the core brand coherent while opening the door to the Experience segment.

### Specific vocabulary risks

- **"Game"** — never use in any Heirlooms-branded surface. Use "experience," "campaign," or "challenge."
- **"Win"** / **"Prize"** — acceptable in Experience segment copy directed at participants, not in any platform-level description.
- **"Competition"** — acceptable in Experience segment; replace with "exclusive access" or "first to unlock" in consumer-facing copy.
- **"Die"** / **"Expire"** (capsule expiry) — the word "die" is never used in any Heirlooms copy. The capsule's window "closes." Content that was not recovered "is no longer available." The brand does not use death as a game mechanic, even when the product mechanic is exactly that.

## Top 10 features (ranked, with rationale)

Ranked by commercial and strategic significance — the combination of addressable market, defensibility, and near-term revenue potential.

**1. Cryptographically enforced time-locked capsule delivery**
The product's core promise made real. No competitor has this. It is the differentiation that sustains every other commercial claim. Without it, Heirlooms is a scheduling tool. With it, Heirlooms is a category of one.

**2. Expiry-as-death (upper bound window)**
The ability for content to cryptographically cease to exist after a defined window is unprecedented in consumer or enterprise software. It opens the Experience segment entirely and adds a layer of trust to the Memory Archive segment (your content is not sitting on a server indefinitely — it has a defined lifecycle). This is the feature most likely to generate press and word-of-mouth.

**3. E2EE with zero-knowledge server**
"Heirlooms cannot read your content. Ever." This is not a marketing claim — it is a cryptographic property. In a market where trust in digital services is declining and data breaches are routine, a zero-knowledge architecture is a genuine moat. It also makes the product defensible against regulatory pressure in ways that operationally-private services are not.

**4. Care & Consent (Care Mode)**
An underserved market with high willingness to pay, a clear emotional trigger (family receiving a diagnosis), and no direct competitor. The combination of cryptographically recorded consent and E2EE care monitoring is legally significant if the Legal persona can validate it. Near-term revenue potential is high; long-term platform value (professional B2B tier for solicitors and care homes) is significant.

**5. Chained capsule DAG**
The mechanic that unlocks the Experience segment. Competitive delivery, expiry-as-death, and QR-to-capsule bridging make this a genuinely novel experience design tool. Near-term addressable market is smaller (ARG designers, agencies) but the case-study value of early adoption is disproportionate to the deal size.

**6. Post-quantum cryptographic architecture**
A 40-year horizon for capsules requires cryptographic agility. The DEK-per-file model and versioned envelope format are the right engineering decisions for this. As a marketing claim, post-quantum positioning is not yet a consumer priority — but it is a differentiator in enterprise and regulated markets, and it will become a consumer story as awareness of quantum computing grows. Filing now and communicating later.

**7. Social recovery via Shamir's Secret Sharing**
Recovery is the trust killer for encrypted products. "What happens if I forget my passphrase?" is the objection that kills conversion for privacy-conscious users. Shamir-based social recovery with a connections graph is both technically elegant and emotionally resonant: the people who hold your recovery shares are the same people who would receive your capsules. This is a user experience design win as much as a cryptographic one.

**8. Friend-tester / PLG model (formalised)**
Not a feature, but a growth mechanic. The friend-tester program as a formalised Founding Member offer is the lowest-cost acquisition channel available. Word-of-mouth from people who have actually sealed a capsule for someone they love is worth more than any paid acquisition channel Heirlooms could afford at this stage.

**9. QR-to-capsule (physical-digital bridging)**
A QR code in a magazine, on a product, or in a book that resolves to a time-gated Heirlooms capsule is a distribution mechanic with no current equivalent. It makes the Experience segment addressable via physical print channels (publishers, brands with packaging budgets) and creates a physical artifact that carries the cryptographic promise into the real world.

**10. The connections graph (executor + recovery + recipients unified)**
The architectural decision to model capsule recipients, Shamir recovery shareholders, and Care Mode executors as a single connections graph is commercially significant. It means that the more deeply a user engages with one segment (e.g. setting up posthumous delivery), the more valuable the product becomes in another segment (e.g. Care Mode setup uses the same connections). This is the network effect that prevents churn: the product becomes harder to leave as the connections graph grows.
