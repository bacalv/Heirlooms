# Sealed at the Same Moment
## Time-guaranteed integrity for legal evidence and disclosure

**Sector:** Legal Evidence, Courts & Litigation  
**Audience:** Litigation partners, barristers, arbitration institutions  
**Version:** 1.0 — May 2026 | Pre-patent safe — may be distributed freely

---

Simultaneous expert production is not simultaneous. It is an agreement between solicitors to open their envelopes at the same time, enforced by nothing more than professional obligation and the practical difficulty of proving otherwise. The opposing expert's report sits on a server somewhere, technically accessible to anyone with the right credentials — or the right relationship with the right person — before the agreed exchange date. The convention works, most of the time, because lawyers are officers of the court and because the risk of being caught is real. But the guarantee is social, not technical.

There is a different kind of guarantee. One that does not depend on trusting the other solicitor, does not depend on server access controls, and does not rely on anyone choosing to behave well under pressure. A guarantee that makes early access mathematically impossible — not against policy, but against the laws of computation.

---

## The problem in simultaneous production

Under the current architecture of simultaneous expert exchange — whether in English High Court proceedings, LCIA arbitration, or ICC-administered disputes — each party's report exists as a readable document from the moment it is completed. The controls that prevent early disclosure are procedural and reputational, not structural.

That gap matters in high-stakes cases. The party whose solicitor obtains even an hour's advance sight of the opposing expert's conclusions has a meaningful advantage: which concessions to withdraw, which lines to harden, which supplementary questions to prepare. The integrity of the process depends entirely on everyone involved choosing not to exercise a capability that technically exists.

This is not a hypothetical concern. It is a structural vulnerability that the profession has accommodated because no technical alternative has been available.

## What changes

This platform enforces simultaneous production in a way that the term has never before meant technically.

When an expert report is sealed into the system, the content becomes cryptographically inaccessible — to opposing counsel, to the platform operator, to anyone with access to the underlying infrastructure. Not access-controlled. Not password-protected. Inaccessible: the means of decryption do not yet exist. They come into existence at the agreed exchange moment, independently of any party to the proceeding, through a process that nobody — including those who built the platform — can accelerate or suppress.

Your expert's report is mathematically inaccessible to opposing counsel until the moment both sides' reports open simultaneously. Not by convention. Not by trusting the other solicitor. By mathematics.

At the point of simultaneous exchange, both reports become readable at precisely the same moment. Neither side could have read the other's report early. This is not an assertion of process compliance. It is a consequence of how the system works.

## The second problem: litigation hold and document retention

Simultaneous production is the most immediately relatable problem, but it is not the only one the platform addresses.

Litigation holds create a document retention obligation that, in practice, rarely has a defined end. Once a hold is placed, the instinct — reinforced by anxiety about future liability — is to retain indefinitely. Documents from concluded matters accumulate. The GDPR retention question is pushed to a later review cycle that never quite happens. The default is to hold everything, forever, and hope the obligation never becomes a problem.

The upper bound that this platform enforces solves that problem structurally, not administratively. When a document is sealed, the window during which it is accessible can be defined at the point of sealing. When that window closes, the means of decryption are permanently destroyed — not archived, not escrowed, not moved to cold storage. Gone. A sealed document whose window has closed cannot be read. Not by a third party, not by the operator, not in response to a future disclosure request for matters that are no longer live.

For litigation hold compliance, this is not a process improvement. It is a different category of solution. The obligation to retain accessible documents ends when the documents cease to be accessible — and that cessation is verifiable, not merely asserted.

## Integrity: the third guarantee

Alongside the time bounds, every document sealed into the platform carries a cryptographic integrity guarantee. Any modification to the content after sealing is detectable. Not detectable with effort, or detectable by a forensic examiner — detectable by the mathematics that governs the seal itself. A document produced from the platform either carries a valid integrity attestation or it does not. There is no middle state.

For expert evidence, this matters in a specific way: the report that opens at exchange is provably the report that was sealed. Not a version of it. Not a file with the same name. The document itself, unmodified, with a verifiable timestamp of when it was sealed. Chain of custody, as a concept, becomes redundant. The integrity is in the artefact, not in the record of who handled it.

For sealed witness statements, the same property applies. A statement sealed prior to a hearing, opened at the court's direction, carries a proof of integrity that no paper-based process can match.

## The institutional dimension

The procedural frameworks that govern simultaneous exchange — the CPR in English proceedings, the rules of LCIA arbitration, the ICC's disclosure protocols — are built around the social trust model, because no technical alternative has existed. None of them require that simultaneous exchange be technically enforced; they require that it be procedurally observed.

This platform does not conflict with those frameworks. It strengthens them. A court or arbitral institution that specifies time-guaranteed simultaneous exchange as a procedural option is not changing its rules. It is offering parties a mechanism that makes compliance with existing rules structurally certain rather than conventionally expected.

The institutions that adopt this first will be the ones that can credibly say: in proceedings administered here, simultaneous production means simultaneous.
