# Sealed Sources
## Time-guaranteed source protection for investigative journalism

**Sector:** Investigative Journalism & Press Freedom  
**Audience:** Senior journalists, editors, news security leads, press freedom organisations  
**Version:** 1.0 — May 2026 | Pre-patent safe — may be distributed freely

---

A journalist is sitting in a café in London. In her bag is a USB drive. On the drive is a cache of documents handed to her three weeks ago by a person who asked for one assurance: that if anything happens before the story runs, it still runs. And that once it does, the documents are gone. Not filed. Not archived. Gone — because the source has to go on living in the country whose officials are named in those files.

She has no way to give that assurance. She can promise. She can delete things manually after publication. But a subpoena does not ask politely, and a National Security Notice served under the UK Investigatory Powers Act 2016 does not come with advance notice. If those files exist anywhere — on a server, in a cloud backup, on a device seized at the border — they can be reached.

She knows this. Her source knew it when they handed over the drive. They trusted her anyway.

That trust is currently a policy decision. It should be a mathematical fact.

---

## The problem is structural, not procedural

Investigative journalism has developed sophisticated operational security: encrypted messaging, air-gapped machines, secure drop systems. These are good tools. They address the question of how a document gets to a journalist. They do not address what happens to it once it arrives.

Once a document is in a newsroom, it exists. It can be compelled. A court injunction granted before a story publishes can prevent publication and preserve the evidence trail. A journalist arrested in transit cannot ensure their editors can access the material. An investigation spanning two years — as complex financial or corruption investigations often do — leaves source materials exposed for that entire duration. And after publication, when the source's usefulness as evidence is spent, the documents often remain: on servers, in archives, in the digital debris of the reporting process.

The UK Investigatory Powers Act gives authorities powers to issue National Security Notices requiring access to data with no judicial pre-authorisation. The Investigatory Powers Tribunal operates largely in secret. Warrant applications targeting journalistic material can be made without notifying the journalist. In this environment, the weakest link is not the source's tradecraft. It is the persistence of the material itself.

The two moments that most damage sources are the same moments a newsroom has the least control: the period before a story is ready to publish, when nothing has been made safe yet, and the indefinite period after publication, when the documents that proved the story remain on record and within reach.

## What a time-sealed press platform changes

A sealed source platform applies time-guaranteed encryption to both of those moments simultaneously.

When a source delivers material, it is sealed with a defined access window: a period that begins at a chosen future date and ends at a chosen close date. Before the window opens, the means of decryption does not exist. Not on a server. Not in a database. Not held in escrow by a platform operator. It has not been generated. No court order, no National Security Notice, no physical seizure of servers produces anything usable — because there is nothing there to produce.

If a journalist is detained before publication, the story does not die with the investigation. The material becomes accessible to designated editors when the window opens — regardless of what has happened to the journalist.

Once the reporting window closes, the means of decryption are permanently destroyed. Not deleted in the ordinary sense of the word. Destroyed, by a process that cannot be reversed and that no party — including the platform operator — can prevent or undo. The documents that underpinned the story are mathematically gone. Future subpoenas find nothing. Future governments, with expanded surveillance powers, find nothing. Future seizures of devices and servers find nothing. Because nothing is there.

Throughout the entire period, the platform stores only ciphertext. The operator cannot read the contents. Cannot be compelled to decrypt. Cannot be made to hand over something they do not have.

## Both bounds are equally important

Press freedom organisations, and the legal frameworks that protect journalism, have focused primarily on the lower bound: keeping material out of the hands of authorities before it is published. Secure drop systems, encrypted channels, and careful operational security all serve that goal.

The upper bound — what happens after publication — has received almost no attention. It is assumed that deletion will do. It will not. Deletion is a statement of intent, not a physical fact. A court order arriving the morning after publication, demanding the preservation and disclosure of source materials, falls on an organisation that still has those materials. Complying may be legally required. The source bears the cost.

A platform where the window has closed and the decryption capability has been destroyed has nothing to hand over. Not because the organisation chose not to cooperate. Because there is genuinely nothing there. That distinction — between a policy of deletion and a mathematical impossibility of recovery — is the difference between a newsroom that tries to protect its sources and a newsroom that structurally cannot betray them, even under the most extreme legal pressure.
