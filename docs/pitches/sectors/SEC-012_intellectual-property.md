# The Undeniable Priority Date
## Time-guaranteed proof of invention for IP protection

**Sector:** Intellectual Property & Patent Protection  
**Audience:** IP counsel, patent attorneys, R&D directors  
**Version:** 1.0 — May 2026 | Pre-patent safe — may be distributed freely

---

An R&D team has spent fourteen months building something genuinely new. They know it works. They have lab notebooks, commit logs, internal presentations, a working prototype. What they do not have is a way to prove, to anyone outside the team, that the invention existed before the date their competitor filed — without showing their competitor what it is.

This problem is older than the patent system. It has never been properly solved.

---

## The gap nobody talks about

IP law is built around a concept that turns out to be surprisingly hard to establish: when did you first have the idea, and can you prove it without disclosing what the idea is?

There are three approaches in common use. None of them are adequate.

**Hash-based timestamps** record that a particular file existed at a moment in time. They prove nothing about what happened to the file afterwards. A timestamp from three years ago proves a file existed three years ago. It does not prove the file has not been modified since. Against a sophisticated dispute, a hash timestamp proves creation date but leaves integrity in question — and integrity is precisely what a priority challenge will target.

**Patent applications** are the conventional answer. They are also slow, expensive, and public. The moment an application is published — typically eighteen months after filing — competitors know what you have built, in enough detail to design around it, challenge it, or accelerate their own work. An application is both the proof and the disclosure. For technology with long development cycles, or for techniques a company would prefer not to reveal until commercialisation, premature filing can cost as much as it protects.

**Defensive publication** solves the patent race by making the technique unpatentable — by anyone. It establishes prior art, which prevents a competitor from filing, but it also prevents the innovating company from filing. The invention enters the public domain. The investment made to create it is surrendered to everyone, including the competitor it was supposed to stop.

There is no current mechanism to establish a cryptographic priority date — one that proves the invention existed and has not been changed — without disclosing what you have built.

## What a sealed IP disclosure platform changes

A time-guaranteed invention record applies two properties simultaneously: a verifiable lower bound on when the content was sealed, and an integrity guarantee covering everything from that moment forward.

When an invention is sealed on the platform, it receives a timestamp from an external process entirely independent of the platform operator. No party — not the R&D team, not the company's legal department, not the platform itself — controls this process or can influence its output. The timestamp cannot be backdated. It is a fact of record established by a mechanism that exists outside any party's reach.

From the moment of sealing, any modification to the content is immediately detectable. The sealed record and the invention it contains are mathematically bound together. Altering the document — even a single character, even in a way designed to be invisible — breaks the verification. A patent examiner, a court, or an opposing counsel presented with the sealed record can confirm in seconds that what they are looking at is identical to what was sealed on the recorded date. The integrity is not an assertion. It is verifiable by any party with access to the record.

The upper bound — the access window — is the third property that no existing approach provides. The sealed disclosure can be configured to expire. Until the patent examiner is granted access during examination, the content of the invention remains sealed. Competitors cannot examine it. It is not published. It does not enter the prior art record. The priority date is established without triggering any of the disclosure consequences that make premature filing a strategic liability.

If the invention is never pursued through formal channels, the window can be allowed to close. The sealed record expires. Nothing becomes public. The R&D investment is protected without a disclosure obligation ever arising.

## What this means for the priority date

A priority date established through a sealed IP disclosure is not a claim about when an invention was created. It is a mathematical fact about when a specific record was sealed and a verifiable proof that the record has remained unchanged since.

In a dispute — an interference proceeding, an opposition, an invalidity challenge — the question is not whether the company says it had the idea first. The question is what can be proven. A sealed record with a third-party timestamp and a verifiable integrity guarantee answers that question at a level of precision no internal document, no matter how carefully maintained, can match.

Patent examiners assessing novelty can be granted time-bounded access to the sealed disclosure during examination. The access window can be defined to open when examination requires it and to close when examination concludes, preventing the record from becoming a public document through the examination process itself. The examiner sees the invention. The competitor does not.

## The cases where this matters most

**Pre-filing strategic planning.** A company with a pipeline of R&D outputs needs to decide which inventions to file, in which jurisdictions, on what timeline. Filing too early discloses too much. Filing too late risks another party establishing prior art. A sealed invention record decouples the priority date from the filing decision. The date is secured now. The filing decision can be made later, with more information, without giving up the priority.

**The invention you cannot yet afford to file.** Patent prosecution across multiple jurisdictions costs tens of thousands of pounds per application. Companies with active R&D programmes make constant triage decisions about which inventions are worth the investment. A sealed record secures the date on inventions that are not yet ready for full prosecution without forcing the choice between filing and losing priority.

**The technique you prefer to keep proprietary.** Some inventions are more valuable as trade secrets than as patents — but trade secrets provide no protection against independent discovery. A competitor who develops the same technique independently can file and potentially threaten the original innovator's ability to use their own invention. A sealed disclosure establishes prior art that prevents this outcome without triggering public disclosure.

**The research programme with a long horizon.** Basic research programmes may take years to reach a commercialisable output. The inventive contribution often occurs long before filing is sensible. A sealed record dated to the moment of conception protects the full development arc.
