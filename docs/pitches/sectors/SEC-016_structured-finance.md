# Simultaneous, by Mathematics
## Time-guaranteed distribution for structured finance

**Sector:** Structured Finance & CLO Administration  
**Audience:** CLO managers, trustees, structured finance legal counsel  
**Version:** 1.0 — May 2026 | Pre-patent safe — may be distributed freely

---

The quarterly trustee report went out at 9:47 a.m. By 9:49 a.m., one note holder had called their broker. The others received their copies at 9:52 a.m. Nothing was wrong with the process. The distribution list was correct. The email went to everyone at the same moment — at least, that is what the logs showed.

Whether it actually arrived at the same moment is a different question. Whether any single recipient had an informational advantage in those two minutes is a question nobody has a rigorous answer to. And in a market where trustee reports constitute material non-public information, "we believe it was simultaneous" has never been a sufficient answer.

---

## What simultaneous actually means

Under securities law, simultaneous distribution of MNPI means exactly what it says. A trustee report that reaches one note holder before another gives that note holder a trading advantage. That is a violation. The mechanism that prevents it — for every CLO, every CDO, every structured vehicle subject to reporting obligations — is currently a policy commitment backed by an email timestamp.

Email delivery is not simultaneous. It passes through queues, servers, spam filters, and network paths that are materially different for different recipients. A message sent to forty addresses at the same instant may arrive across a window of several minutes. In normal commercial correspondence this does not matter. In structured finance reporting, those minutes are the violation.

The industry manages this with careful process: distribution lists locked close to send time, no selective pre-release, legal review of every communication. The discipline is real. But disciplined process is still a human control operating in an environment with no technical guarantee of the outcome it is meant to produce. The audit trail proves that the email was sent. It does not prove that every note holder was able to read it at the same moment.

## The sealed envelope

A time-sealed distribution platform changes the structure of the guarantee entirely.

The mechanism works as follows in terms of its effects. The trustee report is encrypted before it leaves the trustee's environment. Every note holder receives the same sealed document — a package that is inaccessible to all of them equally, regardless of when it arrives in their inbox or portal. The means of decrypting it does not yet exist. It will come into existence at a defined release time, through a process that runs completely independently of the trustee, the CLO manager, and the platform itself.

At the release moment, every note holder — simultaneously, as a property of the underlying mechanism and not as a property of network routing — gains the ability to decrypt. The document they received two days ago, or two hours ago, or two seconds ago becomes readable at the same instant. The arrival time is irrelevant. The reading time is guaranteed.

This is not a policy. It is not a server control that can be misconfigured. It is not a release button that a human hand presses. The release is a mathematical event. It cannot be accelerated for a preferred investor. It cannot be suppressed. No party — not the trustee, not the manager, not the platform operator — has the ability to give any single note holder early access, because there is nothing to hand over before the window opens.

For every note holder on the distribution, the guarantee is the same: they received a sealed document, and it opened at the same moment as everyone else's. That is now auditable. It is provable. It is not a claim about email logs.

## The problem that follows the deal

Simultaneous distribution addresses the live reporting problem. The problem that follows a CLO to its wind-down is different in kind.

When a CLO is closed out, the question of what to do with the archive arises. Waterfall models. Coverage test calculations. Interest diversion analyses. Rating agency correspondence. Working papers from the initial ramp. These documents represent years of operational history, and under most retention schedules they are required to be kept for a period after wind-down. The period ends. The documents are supposed to be destroyed.

In practice, they are not destroyed. They live on backup tapes, in email archives, in the document management systems of the trustee bank and the collateral manager and outside counsel. "Destroyed" in this context has always meant something closer to "we stopped looking at them." It has never meant they do not exist.

The post-2008 CLO litigation history is a case study in what that distinction costs. Documents that all parties believed had been retired surfaced through discovery — waterfall models showing margin calculations that had been informally understood but never meant for public scrutiny, working papers that reflected judgment calls made in the ramp period that looked different in the light of litigation. The exposure was not from documents that were preserved intentionally. It was from documents that were never actually gone.

A time-sealed archive enforces its own destruction.

Documents placed into the archive are encrypted, with a defined window of accessibility that covers the required retention period. When that period ends — not when someone decides it has ended, but when the mathematical moment arrives — the means of decrypting the archive cease to exist permanently. Not revoked. Not locked behind new access controls. Destroyed, through a process that is irreversible and that no party to the deal, to the litigation, or to the regulatory proceeding has any ability to undo.

What remains on any server, in any backup, under any subpoena, is ciphertext. Structurally indistinguishable from random data. There is no working paper to compel. There is no model to surface. The 20-year litigation tail is cut at the point where the retention obligation ends, because at that point the documents stop existing in any form that can be used.

This is not a data governance policy. It is a technical fact.

## What changes for trustees and managers

For trustees, the reporting guarantee removes the weakest link in simultaneous distribution compliance. The current exposure is not usually a disclosure failure — it is the inability to prove that there was no disclosure failure. A sealed distribution produces a different kind of record: not "we sent it to everyone at 9:47" but "every note holder received a sealed document that became readable at 09:47:00 UTC, and no party had the ability to open it before that moment." That record is auditable independently of the trustee's own systems.

For CLO managers, the wind-down archive question has a clean answer for the first time. Retention obligations are met — the archive is accessible for the required period. At the end of that period, destruction is automatic, irreversible, and provable. The liability that accumulates when documents outlive their retention period stops accumulating.

For structured finance counsel, both properties change the posture of the underlying legal risk. MNPI exposure from distribution timing has a structural control behind it, not just a process control. Litigation exposure from survived documents is eliminated at the moment the retention window closes, not managed by hoping that discovery does not surface what should have been gone.

---

## What this is not

It is not a secure portal with enhanced access controls. Secure portals manage who can see a document after it has been decrypted. This mechanism governs whether decryption is possible at all.

It is not a retention management system with automated deletion. Automated deletion removes files from specific servers, on schedules managed by humans who can modify them. This mechanism makes the encrypted content permanently inaccessible across every copy, on every server, regardless of what any party does after the window closes.

It is not a compliance policy. It is a compliance property — something the system provably does, not something the system is configured to attempt.

---

The trustee report has been a policy problem dressed as a solved one for as long as structured finance has existed. Simultaneous is a word that appears in legal opinions and compliance manuals as though its meaning were self-enforcing. It is not, and it never has been.

The wind-down archive problem is older than the 2008 cases that made it visible. Documents outlive their retention periods because deletion has always meant something softer than destruction. The litigation risk that results is not a failure of process — it is a failure of the underlying technology to support what the process requires.

Both of these are solvable. The same underlying mechanism that makes one provable makes the other permanent. The combination, applied to structured finance reporting and archiving, is new. The window to establish it as a defined category, with defined legal meaning, is open now.

Every note holder at the same moment. Every archived document gone when it should be. Not as a policy. As a fact.
