# The Bid You Cannot Read
## Time-guaranteed fairness for property transactions

**Sector:** Real Estate & Property Transactions  
**Audience:** PropTech founders, estate agents, conveyancing platforms  
**Version:** 1.0 — May 2026 | Pre-patent safe — may be distributed freely

---

A couple in Manchester spent eight months looking for a house. They found one they loved — a semi-detached in Didsbury, the right school catchment, the right size, the right price. They made their offer. The vendor's agent told them the process was a sealed bid round. Put your best offer in an envelope by Thursday at noon. Nobody will see it until the deadline. You will be competing fairly against everyone else who wants this house.

They put in a number they had stretched to reach. They did not win. The house sold to a buyer who, they later discovered through a mutual connection, had submitted their bid two hours after getting a phone call telling them what the highest offer currently on the table was.

The agent denied it. There was no way to prove it. There never is.

---

## Sealed bids are not sealed

In England and Wales, sealed bid rounds have no legal teeth. An estate agent can open a bid the moment it arrives. They can read every number, call their preferred buyer, and let them sharpen their pencil. The process is called sealed because bids are submitted in envelopes. The sealing is a social convention — a courtesy that a dishonest agent can ignore without consequence, because there is no mechanism to detect it and no technical barrier to prevent it.

This is not a rare failure. It is the default operating condition of the market. An estate agent's incentives are not straightforwardly aligned with a fair process. Their ongoing relationships — with repeat buyers, with developers, with cash purchasers who will transact quickly and not demand remedial work — create pressure to favour certain outcomes. A genuinely sealed process removes that option. Nobody is building one.

The second injustice follows the first, and strikes harder. A buyer wins a sealed bid round, an offer is verbally accepted, solicitors are instructed, searches are ordered. Two months later, while contracts are still being drafted, a higher offer arrives. The vendor accepts it. The first buyer's offer was never binding. Their legal fees are gone. Their survey costs are gone. Six weeks of belief that they had found their home are gone. This is gazumping, and it is legal, and it is routine.

The gap between "offer accepted" and "contracts exchanged" is a no-man's-land that UK property law has never closed. During that period, the accepted offer has no force. The vendor can walk. The buyer can walk. A competing buyer can sweep in at any point. The market has tried to close this gap with goodwill and gentlemen's agreements. It has failed.

## What time-guaranteed encryption changes

A platform built on time-guaranteed access does something no PropTech product currently on the market does: it enforces both sides of a property transaction by mathematics rather than by trust.

When a sealed bid round opens, bidders submit their offers to the platform. Those offers are encrypted with an access window that begins at the bid deadline — say, Thursday at noon. Before that moment, the means to read any bid does not exist. Not in a database held by the platform. Not accessible to the running agent. Not reachable by any party, including whoever built the system. The agent cannot open bids early. They cannot call a favoured buyer. They cannot compare numbers before the deadline. This is not a policy that depends on the agent's honesty. It is a property of the mathematics. Early access is not prevented; it is impossible.

At the deadline, all bids become readable simultaneously. Every bidder knows this. Every bidder can verify it. The round is sealed in the only sense that matters: by construction, not by convention.

The second guarantee operates at the other end of the transaction. When an offer is accepted, the exchange documents — the agreed terms, the conditions, the completion timeline — are sealed into a window that expires at the agreed completion date. If exchange does not occur within that window, the documents expire automatically. The key material is gone. There is nothing to dispute, nothing to litigate, no solicitor required to unwind a failed escrow. The position reverts cleanly, by the same mathematical process that enforced it.

This is the guarantee the gazumped buyer in Manchester needed. Not a policy. Not a promise from the vendor. A completion window with a hard edge: exchange within this period, or the deal is automatically unwound and both parties are free.

## Both bounds matter equally

Most attempts to improve property fairness focus on one problem or the other. Bid transparency proposals address the sealed bid question. Anti-gazumping reform proposals address the completion gap. Nobody has addressed both, and the reason is clear: the two problems share a single root cause that neither legal reform nor software workflow can reach. The root cause is that there has been no technical mechanism capable of enforcing time boundaries on access to sensitive documents.

The lower bound — bids that are genuinely inaccessible before the deadline — makes the sealed bid process something it has never been before: honest by construction. The agent cannot cheat, not because they have been trained better, not because they have signed a code of conduct, but because the system returns nothing readable until the deadline arrives.

The upper bound — exchange documents that expire if completion does not occur — gives the accepted offer period the structure it has always lacked. It does not require new legislation. It does not require the vendor to sign a lock-out agreement. The window is enforced by the same process that sealed the bid round, and it unwinds automatically if the conditions are not met.

These two guarantees are not competing features. They are two edges of the same instrument. A platform that provides only the lower bound makes the bid process fairer but leaves the post-acceptance period exposed. A platform that provides only the upper bound protects the completion window but leaves the bid round open to manipulation. Together, they cover the transaction from first offer to exchange with a guarantee that no existing product provides.

## The market context

PropTech has made meaningful progress on workflow and transparency in the last decade. Coadjute has built a blockchain-based chain-of-transaction record that lets parties see where a sale stands. Moverly is bringing the material information pack forward in the process, reducing fall-throughs by surfacing problems earlier. Thirdfort has digitised identity verification and AML checks to speed up the early stages. These are genuine improvements to a process that has changed very little since the 1920s.

None of them touch the enforcement problem. Coadjute records what happened; it cannot prevent what should not have happened. Moverly reduces information asymmetry; it cannot prevent a vendor from accepting a higher offer. Thirdfort verifies who you are; it cannot verify that the agent kept your bid sealed.

The enforcement problem requires something none of these platforms has: a mechanism that removes human discretion from the critical moments, not by monitoring it but by making it structurally irrelevant. That mechanism now exists.

## The patent urgency

The approach behind these guarantees — the specific construction that enforces both the lower bound and the upper bound, and the way they are combined into a single transaction envelope — has been checked carefully against academic research and patent filings. Nobody has patented it.

That matters more in a commercial sector like property than it does in almost any other application of this technology. The UK residential property market turns over approximately £300 billion per year. Platforms that become the infrastructure for transaction integrity at that scale are not incremental improvements; they are category-defining. A patent that covers the underlying mechanism is what separates a feature that can be copied by any well-resourced competitor from a defensible position that a strategic acquirer — a major conveyancing platform, a mortgage lender, a national estate agency network — would need to acquire rather than replicate.

UK patent law has no grace period. If the mechanism is described in sufficient technical detail before a priority date is established, or if any other party files first, the right is permanently lost. This document describes only effects — what the platform enforces and why that matters — and is safe to distribute. The mechanism behind those effects is what is being filed. The priority date is the clock that is running.

The couple in Manchester would have paid the same price they paid, if the process had been what it claimed to be. They lost a house they loved, not because someone outbid them, but because someone was allowed to read their bid and ask a favoured buyer to beat it.

A sealed bid round that is genuinely sealed — not by trusting the agent, but by mathematics — would have changed the outcome. Not the market, not the regulations, not the behaviour of that particular agent. Just the bid round. And if the offer had been accepted, a completion window with a hard edge would have given them the protection no verbal acceptance has ever provided.
