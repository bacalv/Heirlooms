# When the Moment Is Safe
## Time-guaranteed protection for transitional justice testimony

**Sector:** Transitional Justice & Reconciliation Archives  
**Audience:** Truth commission directors, international justice bodies, human rights organisations  
**Version:** 1.0 — May 2026 | Pre-patent safe — may be distributed freely

---

People testify before truth commissions because they are promised something. The promise is specific: that what they say will be heard, that it will be protected, and that it will not be turned against them. Transitional justice depends entirely on that promise being credible. When it fails, the harm is not only to the individuals who testified — it is to the entire project of structured accountability.

It has failed. Repeatedly.

---

## What the archive problem actually is

The South African Truth and Reconciliation Commission collected more than 21,000 victim statements. Its archive became a contested object almost immediately — subject to access disputes, political pressure, and questions about how long its protections could last. The Colombian Special Jurisdiction for Peace operates under a peace agreement whose political durability cannot be guaranteed. Rwanda's reconciliation processes produced records whose downstream use has been shaped more by political circumstance than by the promises made to those who contributed.

The problem is not bad intentions. Truth commissions are staffed by serious, principled people. The problem is structural: archives are static, but political circumstances are not. A record that is sealed today can be unsealed when governments change, when international bodies shift their priorities, when legal frameworks are revised, or when the institution that made the original commitment no longer exists. Every protection that depends on institutional continuity will eventually meet a moment when that continuity breaks.

There is a second, quieter problem. Archives that are sealed indefinitely do not actually protect anyone. They create a permanent liability. A sealed record is a future obligation — to review, to decide, to resist pressure — that successor institutions inherit without limit. The people who testified are not protected by indefinite sealing. They are simply deferred.

## What a mathematical guarantee changes

The platform described here applies time-guaranteed cryptographic protection to testimony archives. That requires careful explanation, because the word "encrypted" is used loosely in ways that do not capture what matters here.

Standard encryption protects content from unauthorised access. A sufficiently resourceful adversary — a government, a security service, a future authority with legal compulsion powers — can often compel or circumvent it. The protection is only as strong as the institution holding the keys.

This is different.

Before the defined access window opens, the means to decrypt sealed testimony do not exist anywhere. They have not been generated. There is no key locked in a vault, no escrow holder who can be pressured, no court order that could compel disclosure, because there is nothing to disclose. The protection in this period is not institutional — it is mathematical. A successor government cannot access testimony given under the previous government's truth commission, not because a law prevents it, but because the access mechanism does not yet exist.

When the window opens — when the conditions set at the time of testimony are met — authorised recipients can access what was sealed for them. This is the disclosure period: the moment the commission, the archive, or the designated beneficiaries of the testimony can engage with it.

After the window closes, testimony that was not formally published is permanently destroyed. Not archived in a more secure location. Not transferred to a different institution. Gone. The key material is eliminated. What was sealed but not disclosed in the window becomes mathematically inaccessible — permanently, to anyone, including the platform's own operators.

This is the guarantee that matters. Not "we will protect this for as long as we can." Not "access will be restricted unless compelled." The testimony either exists in a defined window, for defined recipients, or it does not exist at all.

## What this means for testimony

Consider what this offers a witness who is asked to testify before a truth commission in a country where the transitional government is fragile.

They can testify knowing that their account cannot be accessed by any authority — domestic or international — during the period of political vulnerability, because the access mechanism does not exist during that period. There is no archive that can be raided, no database that can be subpoenaed, no sealed record that a future administration can petition to open. The protection does not depend on the commission surviving, or on the peace agreement holding, or on international guarantees remaining in force.

They can also testify knowing that if the window of safety never opens — if the political conditions for safe disclosure are never met — their testimony will not persist indefinitely as a liability. It will be destroyed on a defined date. The promise is not that it will be protected forever. It is that it will either open safely or cease to exist.

For someone who has seen what happens to testimony when archives are misused, that is a materially different offer.

## The design of a time-bounded archive

A transitional justice deployment of this platform would define, at the point of collection, the conditions that govern each piece of testimony:

**Sealed period.** During this window, testimony is inaccessible to everyone. No access is possible by any means. This period might correspond to the term of a transitional government, a period of particular political sensitivity, or a defined timeframe agreed as part of a peace process.

**Disclosure window.** A defined period during which authorised recipients — the commission, a formal archive body, designated international institutions — can access and formally publish testimony. This is the window in which reconciliation work actually happens.

**Destruction.** At the close of the disclosure window, testimony that has not been formally published is permanently destroyed. Not transferred, not re-sealed, not moved to a different tier of restriction. Eliminated.

The archive's integrity during the sealed period does not depend on any institution's continued commitment. It depends on mathematics. That distinction is the entire point.

## Who should consider this

**Truth commission directors** designing new collection processes, particularly in contexts where political stability during or after the commission's mandate cannot be assured.

**The International Criminal Court and allied bodies** where testimony collection intersects with processes that may take decades to reach their disclosure moment, and where witnesses face long-term risk.

**The International Center for Transitional Justice and comparable organisations** advising on archive design in post-conflict settings.

**Human rights NGOs** operating in contexts where they collect testimony that could endanger contributors if accessed prematurely — and where indefinite retention creates its own risk profile.

**Post-conflict governments** seeking to design truth processes that are credible to potential witnesses precisely because the protections offered do not depend on that government's continued existence or good faith.

---

The argument for using this technology in transitional justice is not primarily technical. It is about what makes testimony possible at all. When people are asked to speak about what was done to them or by them, the promise they are given determines whether they will speak honestly, speak at all, or spend the rest of their lives at risk because they did.

A promise backed by a mathematical guarantee is a different kind of promise from one backed by institutional goodwill. In contexts where institutions have failed, that difference is not marginal. It is the whole question.

Testimony that opens only when the conditions for its disclosure are met. Gone, permanently and by design, when they are not.

That is what this platform makes possible.
