# Sealed Until the Moment
## Time-guaranteed integrity for wills and estate planning platforms

**Sector:** Estate Planning, Wills & Will-Writing Platforms  
**Audience:** Will-writing platform operators, estate planning solicitors, banks

---

A solicitors' firm in a market town closed without notice in 2019. The partners had retired. The files — some held for forty years — were transferred to an appointed manager, then partially lost in transit, then partially recovered, then disputed. Among them were sealed wills. Nobody could say with certainty, months later, whether every document that arrived was in the condition it left.

The clients of that firm had done everything right. They had engaged professionals. They had kept their documents in the correct place. They had trusted the system. The system was not mathematical. It was human, and it failed the way human systems fail: not with malice, but with the ordinary disorder of institutions under pressure.

This is the failure mode that professional ethics cannot prevent. And it is not rare.

---

## The problem is not bad actors

The will-writing industry is not riddled with corrupt solicitors. The problem is more fundamental: the guarantee that a will has not been tampered with rests entirely on the professional integrity of the institution holding it, and the practical impossibility of an outsider ever knowing if that integrity was maintained.

When a family challenges a will — and contested wills are rising across all platforms, from high-street solicitors to digital services like Farewill, Co-op Legal, and Kwil — the question they are asking is not usually whether their solicitor was dishonest. It is whether the document they are looking at is the document that was sealed. There is no way to answer that question with certainty. There is only the chain of custody, the word of the institution, and whatever digital or physical audit trail exists. None of these are proof.

The second problem is more intimate: letters of wishes. The personal documents that accompany wills — instructions to executors, messages to specific children, requests that fall outside the formal will itself — are meant to be private until the right moment. In practice, they are seen by solicitors, scanned by clerks, stored in systems without meaningful access controls, and sometimes delivered in the wrong order, to the wrong person, or not at all. A testator cannot currently design the moment of disclosure. They can only hope that the institution follows their instructions. And instructions are not guarantees.

## What a sealed will platform changes

A cryptographic estate archive applies a different kind of protection to both problems simultaneously.

When a will or letter of wishes is sealed into the platform, the means of reading it does not exist. Not in a database. Not on a server. Not held in escrow by the platform operator or any third party. It has not been generated. Before the window opens — which is triggered by verified death, not by a date or an institutional decision — there is nothing to steal, nothing to compel, and nothing to tamper with. Not because it is locked behind strong encryption. Because the key is not there.

This is the lower bound: nothing is accessible before verified death. Not to the solicitor. Not to the family. Not to anyone with a subpoena. Not to the platform operator. Not to anyone.

When death is verified and the window opens, the document becomes accessible to the people designated by the testator. Only them. In the order they were meant to receive it. At the moment it was always intended for.

When probate is complete and the upper bound is reached — at a time the testator defined — the means of decryption are permanently destroyed. Not deleted. Destroyed, through a process that no party, including the platform, can reverse. A future dispute about what the will said, or whether a letter existed, finds nothing to examine. Because there is nothing there.

Throughout the entire period, the platform stores only ciphertext. The operator cannot read the contents. Cannot be compelled to produce something they do not hold. A firm that closes, a data breach at a digital will-writing service, a bad actor with physical access to a server rack — none of these produce anything useful. There is nothing useful to produce.

## The letter that arrived too late

The personal case for this matters as much as the institutional one.

A parent writes a letter to each of their three children. They are written at different stages of the parent's life, with different things to say. The parent seals them with their will and instructs that each child receive their letter privately, in a particular order, after death. This is not an unusual request. It is an ordinary human wish.

In the current system, that wish depends on a clerk reading the instructions correctly, on the executor following them, on the letters having survived storage intact, on the solicitor having maintained the confidence of the sealed envelope. In practice, a sibling may see a letter meant for another. A letter may arrive at the same moment as the formal will, stripping away the private moment the parent designed. A letter may simply not arrive.

A sealed estate archive enforces the disclosure design of the testator as a technical fact. The right person, at the right moment, and no one else before that moment — not as a set of instructions to be followed, but as a constraint that cannot be overridden.
