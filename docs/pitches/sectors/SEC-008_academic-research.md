The results are coming in, and they are not quite what was expected. Not bad — actually, in one reading, rather interesting. A secondary outcome that was always in the dataset is starting to look like the most compelling finding. The researcher pauses. They go back to their pre-registration. What exactly did they write? Were they really predicting a null result on the primary endpoint, or was that always meant to be exploratory? It was months ago. The wording could be clearer.

Nobody is watching. The pre-registration platform accepts amendments. The timestamp will update, but who checks timestamps? And honestly — does this not better reflect what the study was always designed to find?

This is not a story about a bad researcher. It is a story about a human being under pressure, working within a system that offers the appearance of accountability without the structure to enforce it. The replication crisis was not built by fraudsters. It was built, in large part, by exactly this kind of moment — repeated thousands of times, across thousands of studies, in thousands of careers where the stakes were high and the guardrails were made of honour.

## The problem with institutional timestamps

Pre-registration exists because the scientific community recognised, belatedly, that a hypothesis recorded after the results are known is not a hypothesis at all. The Open Science Framework, AsPredicted, and similar platforms were built to anchor hypotheses in time — to create a record that could be checked against the published paper.

They are a genuine improvement. And they are not enough.

The timestamps on existing pre-registration platforms are institutional attestations. A server records that a document was submitted at a certain time. That record is maintained by an organisation, administered by people, and held in databases that can be queried, amended, or in extreme cases overwritten by anyone with sufficient access. The platform's integrity depends on the platform provider's integrity. The timestamp is a claim, not a proof.

An amendment can be filed silently. A prior version can be made harder to find. The workflow for updating a pre-registration on most platforms was not designed around the assumption of adversarial use — because the problem being solved was imagined as forgetfulness and post-hoc rationalisation, not deliberate manipulation. Both happen. The system protects against neither in any absolute sense.

This is not a criticism of the people who built these platforms. It is an observation about the limits of what any institutional system can guarantee. The replication crisis did not happen because researchers were unusually dishonest. It happened because the scaffolding holding the scientific process together was built from social norms rather than structural constraints — and social norms bend under pressure.

## What a sealed pre-registration system does

A time-guaranteed pre-registration platform applies a constraint to a sealed hypothesis that no amendment workflow can circumvent, because the constraint is not procedural. It is mathematical.

Before data collection begins, the researcher seals their hypothesis. From that moment, the seal carries a verifiable timestamp produced by an external process that nobody controls — not the researcher, not the institution, not the platform operator. The timestamp cannot be backdated. It cannot be issued retroactively. It reflects the moment of sealing as a fact of mathematics, not an assertion of a server.

More importantly: after sealing, the hypothesis cannot be modified. This is not a policy preventing modification. It is a property of the content itself. Any alteration — a single changed word, a reframed endpoint, a shifted significance threshold — produces a detectable signature. The integrity of the sealed record is not attested by a custodian. It is verifiable by anyone who holds the sealed document and applies the same mathematical check. Tampering is not merely prohibited. It is visible.

After publication — once the paper is in the world and the research is on the record — the sealed pre-registration ceases to exist. This is not archival. The researcher's unpublished methodology, including any aspects of their approach that were not reported in the paper, is not preserved indefinitely in a database accessible to competitors, funders, or future litigants. It is gone, in a way that no data retention extension or legal hold can reverse. The window on the researcher's private intellectual work closes when the public record opens.

Throughout the entire process — from sealing to publication — the content is end-to-end encrypted. The platform operator sees only ciphertext. There is no readable copy to be accessed by institutional staff, subpoenaed, or accidentally disclosed.

## Why both properties matter equally

Much of the conversation about pre-registration focuses on the timing guarantee: can we prove the hypothesis was fixed before data collection? That is the right question, and the lower bound — the guarantee that the seal predates the data — is the centrepiece of what this platform provides.

But the integrity guarantee is equally important and often treated as secondary.

A timestamp without tamper-evidence is insufficient. A researcher who cannot backdate their pre-registration can still amend the document and then argue that the current version reflects their original intent. The timestamp tells you when the seal was applied. The integrity property tells you whether anything inside the seal has changed. Without both, you have proven timing but not content. With both, you have proven that what was sealed is what was always sealed — and when.

For journal editors reviewing a pre-registration against a submitted manuscript, this distinction is critical. The question is not just "did they pre-register?" but "is what they pre-registered what they say it is?" A sealed record with verifiable integrity answers that question in a way that an editable database record cannot.

For funding bodies assessing grantee compliance with pre-registration requirements, the same distinction holds. The requirement to pre-register exists to ensure that what is claimed was predicted was in fact predicted. A platform that provides mathematical proof of both the timing and the content is a qualitatively different compliance instrument than one that provides an auditable log.

## The reproducibility movement needs infrastructure

The scientific community's response to the replication crisis has been, broadly, the right one. Pre-registration. Registered reports. Open data. Adversarial collaboration. Replication studies. These are genuine improvements to scientific practice, and the institutions driving them — the Center for Open Science, the UK Reproducibility Network, the broader open science movement — deserve credit for moving a deeply conservative culture.

What has been missing is infrastructure that makes the guarantees structural rather than social. Every current pre-registration system ultimately relies on researchers choosing not to amend their hypotheses after seeing results. The system expresses a norm. It does not enforce a constraint.

A sealed pre-registration platform does not require researchers to be more honest than they are. It removes the option. This is a better foundation for a reproducible science, not because researchers cannot be trusted, but because the structural integrity of a norm is always weaker than the structural integrity of a technical fact — and the scientific process deserves the strongest foundation available.
