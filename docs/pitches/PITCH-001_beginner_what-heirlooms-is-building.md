# Heirlooms

## What it is, why it matters, and why nothing like it exists yet

**Version:** 2.0 — May 2026 | Pre-patent safe — may be distributed freely

---

Imagine receiving a message from someone you love, on a day that mattered to both of you, written years before that day arrived. Not found in an email draft. Not forwarded by a solicitor. Just there — on the day, waiting — sealed the way a letter is sealed, private until the moment it was always meant for.

That is the experience Heirlooms is designed to make possible. And the fact that no reliable way to do it has ever existed is a gap so obvious, once you notice it, that it is hard to understand why.

---

## The problem nobody has solved

Most of us have taken a photo or video of someone we love and thought: *I want them to have this one day.* A parent sealing a message for a daughter's eighteenth birthday, written when she is eight. A grandparent recording a story for grandchildren they may never meet. A message made by someone who knows they are ill, for the people they are not sure they will still be with.

These are not unusual ideas. Almost everyone has had some version of this thought. But there is currently no reliable way to act on them. Cloud storage tells you where to put your files; it has no answer to the question of who should receive this, and when. The services that try — email schedulers, legacy features added to social media platforms — are operationally dependent on a company surviving and choosing to act. They are promises, not guarantees.

Heirlooms is building the thing that should exist but doesn't.

---

## What Heirlooms does

Heirlooms is a personal archive where you can place a photo, a video, or a message and know it will reach the right person at the right moment — with a guarantee that goes beyond trust.

Using it is straightforward: you choose what to include, who should receive it, and when. Then you seal it. The product does the rest.

The guarantee behind that simplicity is something genuinely new. When you seal something in Heirlooms, neither the key needed to open it nor any usable part of it exists anywhere. Not in a database, not locked in a safe, not on a server. It has not been generated. It will come into existence on the date you chose, through a process that runs completely independently of Heirlooms — a process that nobody, including the people who built the product, has any ability to accelerate or suppress.

Before that date, there is nothing to steal, nothing to compel, and nothing to hand over. Because there is nothing there.

And when the time window closes — once the delivery has been made and the period you defined has passed — the means of reconstructing the key are permanently gone. Not locked away. Destroyed. This is the strongest guarantee achievable with today's technology. It is not a policy or a promise. The lower guarantee — that nothing can be opened before the date — is absolute, a property of mathematics. The upper guarantee — that nothing can be opened after the window closes — is the strongest achievable without quantum infrastructure: it requires a network of independent parties to each destroy their portion at the designated time, which they are formally obligated to do.

To use a physical analogy: most existing services are like a security guard who promises to keep your safe locked. Heirlooms is like a safe whose combination does not exist until the dial reaches the right date — and whose inner workings dissolve once the moment has passed.

No consumer product has done this before.

## Why the patent matters

The specific method behind these guarantees — the way the lower and upper bounds are constructed and combined — has been checked carefully against academic research and patent databases. Nobody has patented it.

That matters for two reasons. First, it means Heirlooms can file for protection now. A patent does not just prevent copying — it makes the company significantly more valuable to any future buyer. A care technology company, an insurance group, or a legal services firm that wants this capability would rather acquire the company that holds the patent than build it from scratch. The patent is what creates that option.

Second, the window to file is limited. Patent law in the UK has no grace period: if anyone else publishes or files on the same method first — or if Heirlooms describes the mechanism publicly before filing — the right is permanently lost. The application is being prepared now. The priority date is the critical step.

## Where else this matters

The same guarantee that seals a family message turns out to be useful in a number of other places.

**Families caring for someone with dementia.** There are nearly a million people in the UK living with dementia. Many of them want to record their wishes — about care, about finances — while they still have full capacity to do so. A sealed record with a verifiable timestamp that cannot be changed after the fact is exactly what that situation needs. No existing product provides this with a genuine guarantee of integrity.

**Anyone planning for a possible loss of capacity.** This extends well beyond dementia. Anyone who wants to record what they want to happen if they are ever unable to speak for themselves — whether due to illness or any condition that affects future capacity — faces the same need. A record sealed with a verifiable date, impossible to alter, is a stronger version of what a paper form tries to be.

**Sealed legal documents.** Solicitors hold sealed documents for decades — instructions to executors, conditions on inheritances, letters of wishes. A mathematically sealed document is more permanent than an envelope in a filing cabinet, does not depend on a law firm staying in business, and cannot be opened by anyone who gains physical access.

These are active, unsolved problems. Each of them is addressable with the same underlying approach.

## The product already works

Heirlooms is not a proposal. It is a working product in active use.

The platform runs on cloud infrastructure and is available on Android and web, with an iOS application in development. All content stored today is end-to-end encrypted — the company's own staff, with full access to every server and database, would see only ciphertext they cannot read. This is not a policy. It is a technical fact built into the architecture.

The time-sealed capsule feature — the part that is being patented — is the next major development milestone. What is already live is the encrypted personal archive, multi-user sharing, and the full cloud infrastructure on which capsule delivery will run. The foundation is built; the time-lock is what is being added next.
