## Narrative threads worth developing

These are the themes that could form the backbone of a founder story, a talk, or a press piece:

**"The contractor who came home"**  
Fifteen years selling expertise to other people's visions. The independent, technically capable professional who knows exactly how to build things — but always for someone else. Then, at some inflection point, turning that expertise inward. This is a classic founder origin: not a young graduate with a hot idea, but a seasoned practitioner who waited until he had both the skill and the clarity of purpose. The emotional texture of that transition — from hired gun to founder — is compelling and underexplored in startup narratives.

**"The quiet builder"**  
No public talks. No blog. No prominent GitHub presence. A private person who has spent the years building skills, not profile. And then, alone, building a product of genuine technical sophistication — E2EE, post-quantum-ready, multi-platform, with novel cryptographic IP. The contrast between the public invisibility and the private ambition is a strong narrative.

**"Security as care"**  
Most E2EE products are built by people motivated by ideology (privacy absolutism) or fear (surveillance capitalism). Heirlooms' E2EE is motivated by something different: *care*. The cryptographic guarantees — "Heirlooms cannot read your capsule, ever" — are in service of a promise to the living and the dead. A developer who chose to make the hard engineering choices in service of an emotional product promise is a different kind of character than the typical security engineer.

**"Time as the product"**  
The ROADMAP describes the key insight — the reframe from "where do I put my files?" to "who should receive this memory, and when?" This is an architectural insight as much as a product insight, and it has genuine philosophical weight. A developer who thinks about time as a first-class element in systems (JUXT's functional programming orientation does exactly this) building a product whose entire premise is the gap between now and a future moment of meaning.

**"Building for the people you love"**  
If Bret has a family, and if the product's most emotionally resonant use cases (the 18th birthday video, the posthumous letter) map to his own life in some way, this is the simplest and most powerful story: a father or partner building the product he wishes existed, for the people he most wants to protect.
